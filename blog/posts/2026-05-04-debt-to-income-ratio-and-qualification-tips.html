Debt-to-income ratio and qualification tips

Sunday, May 4, 2026 • Michael Miller

Your debt-to-income ratio (DTI) is one of the first things lenders look at. It's the percentage of your gross monthly income that goes toward debt payments—and it directly affects how much you can borrow and what rates you'll qualify for.

What counts as debt?

  • Mortgage payment (including taxes, insurance, HOA if applicable)
  • Car loans
  • Student loans
  • Credit card minimum payments (even if you carry no balance)
  • Personal loans
  • Child support or alimony

How lenders use DTI. Most conventional loans want your back-end DTI (new mortgage plus all other debts) to be 43% or lower. Some lenders go up to 50% with strong compensating factors like substantial savings or a high credit score. FHA loans are often more flexible—up to 50% DTI, sometimes higher with manual underwriting.

The math. Say your gross monthly income is $5,000. At 43% DTI, your total monthly debt payments (including the new mortgage) can't exceed $2,150. If your car loan is $350, credit cards are $100, and student loans are $200, that's $650 in existing debt. Your mortgage payment can only be about $1,500 to stay at 43%.

Real-world impact. A higher DTI can mean a lower loan amount, higher rates, or both. It also limits your flexibility for future borrowing. But here's the good news: you have options.

Ways to improve your DTI before applying.

  • Pay down credit cards — Even carrying a $5,000 balance on available credit counts toward your DTI (as a payment). Paying it off can free up room immediately.
  • Eliminate small debts — Paying off a car loan or personal loan removes that payment entirely and can unlock a bigger mortgage.
  • Wait 6 months — Student loan payments tend to drop after 6 months off forbearance. Same with some personal loans as balances decline.
  • Increase income — If you can show a raise or side income, lenders will typically count it after 2 years of documentation.
  • Lower the purchase price — Less house means a lower mortgage payment and lower DTI. Sometimes the smartest move is buying less than you're pre-approved for.

What if you're at the limit? You still have a shot. Lenders have "compensating factors"—a higher credit score, a large down payment, substantial savings, or a stable job history can help offset a DTI that's slightly high. Automated systems might reject you, but manual underwriting by a human loan officer can make a difference.

Bottom line. DTI isn't just a number; it's a signal of financial health. The lower yours, the stronger your application and the better your rates. If you're shopping for a mortgage or thinking about refinancing, understanding your DTI puts you in control.
