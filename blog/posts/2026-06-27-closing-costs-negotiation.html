Closing costs negotiation: what you can and can't haggle

June 27, 2026 · 6 min read

Closing costs typically run 2-5% of your purchase price. On a $300,000 home, that's $6,000-$15,000. Most buyers think these costs are set in stone. They're not. Let's walk through what you can negotiate and how to save money at closing.

What are closing costs?

Closing costs are fees charged by various parties involved in your mortgage and home purchase: the lender, title company, appraiser, inspector, government, and others. They're itemized on your Loan Estimate (given at application) and final Closing Disclosure (given 3 days before closing).

Costs you CAN negotiate

Loan origination fees and discount points – These are your lender's profit. Compare Loan Estimates from multiple lenders. A lender offering 1% origination vs. 0.5% is worth $1,500 on a $300,000 loan.

Loan estimate vs. actual closing costs – By law, most lender fees can't increase more than 10% from the Loan Estimate to the Closing Disclosure. If they do, push back in writing.

Title insurance – Shop around. Title insurance companies vary in price, and your lender doesn't own the title company (in most states). Get quotes from 2-3 title companies.

Home inspection and appraisal – Competition exists here. Your lender might recommend vendors, but you can shop independently. Appraisals typically cost $400-600; inspections $300-500. Worth comparing.

Homeowners insurance** – Lock in your rate before closing. Shop 3+ insurers. Rates vary significantly.

Costs you CAN'T negotiate

Property taxes and HOA fees – These are set by your local government and HOA. Non-negotiable.

Recording fees and government charges – Fixed by the county. Non-negotiable.

FHA mortgage insurance premium (upfront) – Mandated by federal law for FHA loans. Non-negotiable.

Escrow deposits – These are your own money held by the title company for taxes and insurance. The amount is based on estimates, so review it, but it's not a "fee."

Strategy 1: Shop multiple lenders

Get Loan Estimates from at least 3 lenders. By law, you have the right to request quotes from as many as you want, and your credit score impact is minimal if you do them all within 45 days. Comparing origination fees, discount points, and closing costs can save $1,000-3,000 easily.

  • Ask each lender: "What's your all-in cost to close?"
  • Compare the same loan type (30-year fixed, same down payment, same loan amount)
  • Factor in the interest rate too—a 0.1% lower rate might offset slightly higher fees

Strategy 2: Ask the seller to cover closing costs

In a buyer's market, you can sometimes ask the seller to pay part of your closing costs (called "seller concessions"). Limits vary by loan type, but it's often 2-6% of the purchase price.

  • This negotiation happens during the offer stage, not at closing
  • If the seller agrees, the credit reduces your costs dollar-for-dollar
  • Note: This can affect your loan approval if the appraisal or your debt-to-income ratio is tight, so coordinate with your lender

Strategy 3: Request a lender credit

If you're comfortable with a slightly higher interest rate, some lenders will credit you closing costs. For example, accepting 7.0% instead of 6.9% might net you a $2,000-3,000 lender credit toward closing costs.

  • This only makes sense if you plan to keep the loan for many years (so the tiny rate difference doesn't cost you more overall)
  • Calculate: Is the closing cost savings worth the extra interest over 30 years?

Strategy 4: Avoid unnecessary services

  • Lender-required inspections: Appraisals are required, but your lender typically can't require specific inspection services. You can often opt out or choose cheaper alternatives.
  • Buy-down services: Ask if paying points upfront is worth your break-even timeline.
  • Loan origination services: Some lenders charge for "processing" or "underwriting." Compare lender-to-lender to see where this is highest.

Strategy 5: Review the Closing Disclosure carefully

You receive the Closing Disclosure at least 3 business days before closing. This is your final itemization. Review it against your Loan Estimate and make sure nothing changed unexpectedly.

  • Look for fees that weren't on the Loan Estimate
  • Check that lender fees didn't increase by more than 10%
  • Call anything suspicious immediately—your lender must justify increases or remove them

Realistic savings targets

Here's what you can reasonably expect:

  • Shopping lenders: Save $1,000-3,000 by comparing origination fees and closing costs
  • Title insurance shopping: Save $200-500
  • Seller concessions: Save $6,000-15,000+ (depends on negotiations)
  • Reviewing for errors: Save $100-500 by catching and disputing overcharges

Timeline: when to negotiate

  • Before making an offer: Get pre-approved with multiple lenders and know your costs
  • When making an offer: Request seller concessions if the market allows
  • At application: Get Loan Estimates from at least 3 lenders
  • 3 days before closing: Review the final Closing Disclosure and dispute any surprises

Final thoughts

Closing costs aren't random—they're the sum of many individual services, some negotiable and some not. By shopping lenders, understanding what can be haggled, and staying vigilant through the closing process, you can realistically save thousands. Most buyers save $1,000-3,000 just by getting competitive quotes.
