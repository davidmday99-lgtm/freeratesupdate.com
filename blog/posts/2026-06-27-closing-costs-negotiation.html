Closing costs negotiation: what you can and can't haggle

June 27, 2026 · 5 min read

Closing costs typically run 2-5% of your home purchase price—that's $6,000-$15,000 on a $300,000 home. While you can't negotiate away all of them, many costs are far more flexible than you think. Here's your complete guide to which ones you can haggle and which ones are locked in.

Costs you can absolutely negotiate

Lender fees (loan origination, processing, underwriting): These vary wildly by lender and are absolutely negotiable. Get quotes from 3-4 lenders and use them to negotiate. A lender seeing you're serious about other options will often drop fees.

Discount points: Completely optional. You're not required to buy down your rate. If a lender is pushing points, negotiate them away.

Title insurance: While the cost is set by the state, some lenders add unnecessary premiums. Compare quotes from multiple title companies.

Recording fees: Typically paid to the county, but some lenders tack on processing. Get a quote breakdown.

Overnight delivery fees: Absolutely negotiate these away. Use regular mail.

Costs the seller can pay (in many states)

A strategic negotiating tactic: ask the seller to cover certain costs. In most markets, sellers pay realtor commissions, but can also be asked to cover:

  • Lender title insurance
  • Attorney/escrow fees
  • HOA transfer fees
  • Some appraisal costs
  • Some inspection-related repairs

This shifts costs from your pocket to the seller's. Builders often do this automatically. Negotiating this during your offer can be more effective than price negotiation.

Fixed costs (usually non-negotiable)

Property taxes: Set by the jurisdiction. Non-negotiable. You pay your portion at closing.

Homeowners association (HOA) fees: Set by the HOA. You're responsible once you buy.

Homeowners insurance: Required by your lender, but you can shop for the best rate before closing.

Attorney fees: In some states, attorney costs are set. In others, they vary. Shop around.

Appraisal fee: Largely standardized ($400-$600) and difficult to negotiate, though some lenders offer them free to attract business.

The Loan Estimate: your first negotiating tool

When you apply for a mortgage, you receive a Loan Estimate within 3 business days. This document breaks down all projected closing costs. Use it to:

  • Compare across lenders (apples-to-apples)
  • Identify which costs vary most by lender (these are negotiable)
  • Challenge any fees that seem out of line
  • Ask about fee waivers or credits

Smart negotiation strategies

1. Get multiple Loan Estimates: Nothing motivates a lender like knowing you have other options. Most will beat their competitors' fees.

2. Shop early: Don't wait until you're under contract. Pre-shopping gives you leverage.

3. Ask about credits: Many lenders offer rate credits or cost credits to stay competitive.

4. Bundle services: If the lender also sells homeowners insurance or title insurance, some will bundle deals.

5. Ask what can be waived: Simple question: "Which of these fees can you waive or reduce?" Often the answer is "most of them."

The Closing Disclosure

Three days before closing, you receive a Closing Disclosure. This is your final cost document. If it differs significantly from your Loan Estimate, you have the right to:

  • Ask for an explanation of every change
  • Request additional time to review (you get 3 days minimum)
  • Close a different day if costs are materially different
  • Work with your lender to fix errors or negotiate changes

The builder advantage

New construction buyers often get better deals because builders:

  • Have preferred lender relationships with better rates
  • Often cover lender fees as incentives
  • Absorb some closing costs to close deals
  • Offer closing cost buydowns or credits

Bottom line

You can expect to negotiate 30-50% off the total lender-controlled closing costs by shopping hard and asking the right questions. That could mean saving $1,500-$3,000 easily. Always ask—the worst they can say is no, but many lenders will negotiate to win your business.
