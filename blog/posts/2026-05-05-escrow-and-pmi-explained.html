Escrow and PMI explained

Tuesday, May 5, 2026 • Michael Miller

When you get a mortgage, you'll encounter two costs that aren't your principal and interest: escrow and PMI. Both protect the lender, but they work very differently. Here's what you need to know.

Escrow: Your safety deposit account

Think of escrow as a holding account. Your lender collects funds each month to cover taxes and insurance—both critical to protecting the collateral (your home). Here's the breakdown:

  • Property taxes: The lender estimates your annual taxes, divides by 12, and collects that amount monthly. Most homeowners pay $2,000–$8,000 annually depending on location and home value.
  • Homeowners insurance: Same approach. A typical policy runs $800–$2,000 per year. The lender requires this to protect against fire, theft, and weather damage.
  • PMI (if applicable): With down payments under 20%, PMI gets rolled into your escrow account.

When taxes or insurance come due, the lender pays directly from escrow. You never write those checks—they're bundled into your monthly mortgage payment.

PMI: Private Mortgage Insurance

PMI protects the lender if you default. It's required when your down payment is less than 20% of the home's purchase price. Here's what varies:

  • Cost: Typically 0.4%–1.5% of your loan amount annually. On a $300,000 loan, that's $1,200–$4,500 per year, or $100–$375 per month.
  • Type: Mortgage insurance can be monthly (PMI), financed upfront (called MIP with FHA loans), or a combination.
  • Duration: Once you reach 20% equity, you can request cancellation. This might take 10–15 years, depending on home appreciation and how fast you pay down principal.

FHA loans charge mortgage insurance for the life of the loan if your down payment is less than 10%—something to factor in if you're comparing loan programs.

How much will you actually pay?

Let's say you're buying a $350,000 home with 10% down ($35,000). Your loan is $315,000.

  • PMI: ~$2,520–$4,725 per year ($210–$395/month)
  • Escrow (estimated): ~$400/month for taxes + insurance combined (varies by state)
  • Total extras: $610–$795 per month before principal and interest

Can you avoid or reduce these costs?

  • PMI: Bring 20% down to skip it entirely. If not possible now, ask about accelerating your payoff to hit 20% equity faster.
  • Escrow: Most lenders require it if your down payment is under 20%. Once you reach 20% equity and eliminate PMI, you may be able to stop escrow, though some lenders hold firm.
  • Refinancing: If home values rise or you pay down principal quickly, refinancing into a conventional loan (dropping PMI entirely) might make sense.

Understanding these costs upfront helps you budget realistically and spot opportunities to save. When comparing mortgage offers, always ask for a complete breakdown of your monthly payment—principal, interest, taxes, insurance, and PMI. That total payment is what impacts your qualifying ratio and your monthly cash flow.
