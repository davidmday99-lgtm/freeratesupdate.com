Understanding mortgage points: when to buy down your rate

June 4, 2026 • Michael Miller

Mortgage points—often called "discount points"—let you pay money upfront to reduce your interest rate. One point equals 1% of your loan amount and typically lowers your rate by 0.25%. The real question: is it worth it?

The basic math

  • 1 point on a $400,000 loan = $4,000 upfront
  • Rate reduction: roughly 0.25% (varies by lender and market)
  • Monthly savings: depends on loan size—about $60-70/month on that $400K example
  • Break-even: divide cost by monthly savings (e.g., $4,000 ÷ $65 = ~61 months)

When buying points makes sense:

  • You're staying in the home 7+ years (past the break-even point)
  • You have extra cash beyond your down payment and closing costs
  • You want the lowest possible monthly payment for cash flow reasons
  • Current rates are relatively high and you're not planning to refinance soon

When to skip the points:

  • You might move or refinance within 5 years
  • That money could go toward a larger down payment (often better ROI)
  • You'd rather keep reserves for repairs, emergencies, or investments
  • Rates are volatile and you might refinance if they drop significantly

Tax angle: Mortgage points are typically tax-deductible in the year you pay them (for a purchase). On refinances, you usually deduct them over the loan term. Check with a tax professional—this can shift the math in your favor.

The hidden option: Negative points (lender credits) work in reverse. You accept a slightly higher rate, and the lender gives you cash toward closing costs. If you're tight on upfront cash or plan to refinance soon, this can be smarter than paying for discount points.

Real-world tip: Ask your lender for a side-by-side comparison: same loan with 0 points, 1 point, and maybe 2 points. Look at the total cost over 5, 10, and 30 years. The "right" answer depends on your timeline and how you value cash now vs. savings later.
