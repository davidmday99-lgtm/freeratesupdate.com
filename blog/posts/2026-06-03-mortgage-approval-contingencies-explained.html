Mortgage approval contingencies explained

June 3, 2026 • Michael Miller

Getting a mortgage pre-approval is just the first step. Your final loan approval comes with contingencies—conditions that must be met before funding. Understanding these contingencies helps you close smoothly and avoid last-minute surprises.

Why contingencies exist:

  • Lenders need to verify your financial situation hasn't changed since pre-approval
  • They must ensure the property meets lending standards
  • Regulations require ongoing verification throughout the process
  • Contingencies protect both you and the lender from unexpected issues

Common approval contingencies:

  • Appraisal contingency: The home must appraise at or above the purchase price. If it doesn't, you'll need to make up the difference in cash, renegotiate the price, or walk away.
  • Financing contingency: You must maintain your financial qualifications (credit score, debt-to-income ratio, employment) through closing.
  • Underwriting conditions: Specific documents or explanations the underwriter requires (gift letters, employment verification, etc.).
  • Title contingency: Clear title must be confirmed with no unexpected liens or ownership issues.
  • Insurance contingency: You must secure homeowners insurance that meets lender requirements.

How to handle contingencies successfully:

  • Stay financially stable: Don't make large purchases, change jobs, or take on new debt between pre-approval and closing
  • Respond quickly to requests: When your loan officer asks for documents, provide them within 24 hours
  • Keep all accounts active: Don't close credit cards or bank accounts during the process
  • Budget for appraisal gaps: Have a plan if the home appraises low—can you bring extra cash or negotiate?
  • Get insurance early: Shop for homeowners insurance as soon as you have a purchase agreement

Red flags that trigger additional contingencies:

  • Recent job change or gap in employment
  • Large deposits or withdrawals you can't explain
  • Credit inquiries or new accounts opened during the process
  • Property issues discovered during inspection that affect value
  • Condominium or HOA issues that affect financing

Timeline management: Most contingencies must be resolved within specific timeframes. Your purchase agreement typically gives you 30-45 days to satisfy all contingencies. Work backward from your closing date to ensure you meet all deadlines.

The final approval process: Even after all contingencies are satisfied, lenders do a final verification 1-3 days before closing called a "loan audit" or "pre-closing compliance check." They'll re-pull your credit and verify no new debts have been incurred.

Next step: Get pre-approved now and ask your loan officer for a complete list of potential contingencies based on your specific situation. Understanding what to expect makes the process smoother and less stressful.
