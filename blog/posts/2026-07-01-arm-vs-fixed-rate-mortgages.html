ARM vs fixed-rate mortgages: which is right for you?

July 1, 2026 · 6 min read

One of the most important mortgage decisions is choosing between a fixed-rate mortgage (same rate for the life of the loan) and an adjustable-rate mortgage (ARM, where your rate changes over time). This choice affects not just your monthly payment, but your financial stability for years to come. Let's break down both options so you can decide which fits your situation.

Fixed-rate mortgages: predictability and security

A fixed-rate mortgage locks in your interest rate for the entire loan term (typically 15, 20, or 30 years). Your rate never changes.

  • Interest rate: Remains the same for the loan's entire duration
  • Monthly payment: Always the same (except for changes in property taxes/insurance in escrow)
  • Budgeting: Easy to predict and plan finances
  • Current rates: Typically 5.75%-6.5% depending on credit and market
  • Best for: Long-term homeowners who plan to stay 10+ years

Adjustable-rate mortgages (ARMs): initial savings with future uncertainty

An ARM starts with a lower "teaser" rate that's fixed for a period (typically 3, 5, 7, or 10 years), then adjusts annually after that, usually tied to a market index.

  • Initial rate: Lower than fixed rates (currently 5.0%-5.5% for 5/1 ARMs)
  • Adjustment: After initial period, rate adjusts yearly based on market index + lender margin
  • Rate caps: Most ARMs have caps limiting how much rates can increase per adjustment and over the loan's lifetime
  • Monthly payment: Increases when rate adjusts (potentially significantly)
  • Best for: Short-term homeowners planning to sell or refinance within the initial fixed period

How ARMs work: a detailed example

5/1 ARM at 5.2%:

  • Years 1-5: Your rate is fixed at 5.2% ($300,000 loan = $1,558/month)
  • Year 6 and beyond: Rate adjusts annually. If it rises to 6.2%, your payment jumps to $1,798/month (+$240)
  • Cap example: 5/1/6 caps mean 5% initial rate, 1% annual adjustment cap, 6% lifetime cap above starting rate
  • Worst case: With a 5/1/6 ARM starting at 5%, the maximum rate over the loan's life is 11% (5 + 6)

ARM risk: payment shock

The real danger of ARMs is payment shock—the sudden jump in your monthly payment when the initial period ends.

Example: $300,000 loan

  • 5/1 ARM at 5.2%: $1,558/month (years 1-5)
  • Rate rises to 7.0% in year 6: $1,998/month (+$440/month or +28%)
  • 7-year additional cost: $440 × 12 × 7 = $36,960 in additional interest

Can you afford that jump? Most homebuyers don't plan for it, leading to financial stress or defaults.

When ARMs make sense

Short-term horizon (planning to sell within 5-7 years):

  • Take advantage of lower initial rates and sell before rates adjust
  • Save $100-200/month during your ownership
  • Avoid the payment shock entirely

Expected significant income increase:

  • If you expect your income to rise significantly after year 5, you can afford the payment increase
  • Example: Medical resident starting residency (low pay now, high pay in 3 years)

Planning to refinance:

  • Lock in the initial low rate for 5-7 years, refinance before rates adjust
  • Only works if rates are lower in the future (impossible to predict)

When fixed-rate mortgages are better

Planning to stay 10+ years:

  • You'll likely hit the adjustment period
  • You want payment stability
  • The savings aren't worth the risk

Limited income flexibility:

  • You can only afford today's payment
  • A 30% payment increase would be catastrophic
  • Fixed-rate provides certainty you can rely on

Uncertain about your plans:

  • You don't know if you'll stay or move
  • Fixed-rate eliminates the gamble

Current market context:

  • Rates already moderate (not historically low)
  • Savings from ARM are minimal (0.5% - 0.75%)
  • Risk/reward ratio favors fixed-rate

The math: when does an ARM make sense?

To decide if an ARM is worth considering, calculate your break-even point:

  • 5/1 ARM: $5.2%, $1,558/month
  • 30-year fixed: 6.0%, $1,799/month
  • Monthly savings: $241
  • 5-year savings: $241 × 60 = $14,460

If you stay past 5 years, you lose this advantage (and then some) when rates adjust. Only choose the ARM if you're highly confident you'll move or refinance.

Hybrid options

Some lenders offer creative compromises:

  • Conversion ARM: Option to convert to fixed-rate during initial period (usually costs a fee)
  • Extended fixed period: 10/1 ARM instead of 5/1 (longer initial period, higher initial rate)
  • Capped ARM: Lifetime rate cap limits maximum increase

Fixed vs ARM in today's market (July 2026)

With fixed rates around 5.75%-6.25% and 5/1 ARMs around 5.0%-5.5%, the savings are modest (0.5-0.75%). The 0.5% ARM advantage doesn't justify the risk for most homebuyers. **Unless you're certain you'll sell within 5 years, a fixed-rate mortgage is the safer choice.**
