ARM vs fixed-rate mortgages: which is right for you?

July 1, 2026 · 6 min read

When shopping for a mortgage, you'll encounter a fundamental choice: lock in a fixed interest rate for the life of the loan, or take an Adjustable Rate Mortgage (ARM) and accept rate changes down the road. Both have merits, but for most homebuyers, one is clearly safer. Let's break it down.

The fixed-rate mortgage explained

A fixed-rate mortgage locks your interest rate for the entire term (usually 15, 20, or 30 years). Your monthly principal and interest payment never changes.

  • Advantages:
    • Payment certainty—budget predictably for 30 years
    • Protects against rising rates—if rates jump to 9%, you're still at 6.8%
    • Peace of mind—no surprises
    • Easier to plan refinancing—you know when your lock ends
  • Disadvantages:
    • Higher starting rate—fixed rates are typically 0.3-0.7% higher than initial ARM rates
    • If rates drop, you must refinance to benefit (costs ~$2,000-5,000)

The ARM (Adjustable Rate Mortgage) explained

An ARM starts with a lower teaser rate for an initial period (typically 3, 5, 7, or 10 years), then adjusts periodically based on market conditions. Your payment can change significantly.

  • Structure: A 5/1 ARM means 5 years fixed, then adjusts annually after that. A 7/6 means 7 years fixed, then adjusts every 6 months.
  • Rate adjustment: Based on a market index (like SOFR or PRIME) plus a lender margin. E.g., SOFR at 5.2% + 2.75% margin = 7.95% rate.
  • Caps: Most ARMs have rate caps that limit how much the rate can increase per adjustment and over the life of the loan.
  • Advantages:
    • Lower starting rate—ARMs typically start 0.3-0.7% lower than fixed rates
    • Monthly savings early on—at current rates, a 5/1 ARM might be 6.2% vs. 6.8% fixed
    • If rates stay flat or drop, you keep the savings (unlike a refinancing cost)
  • Disadvantages:
    • Rate risk—after the fixed period, your rate adjusts and payment can skyrocket
    • Budgeting uncertainty—you don't know what your payment will be in 5-10 years
    • Payment shock—a 2-3% rate increase could add $200-400/month or more to your payment
    • Refinancing trap—if rates have risen, you may be stuck with a higher ARM rate

Real-world comparison: 5/1 ARM vs. 30-year fixed

$300,000 loan, current rates:

  • 5/1 ARM at 6.2%: ~$1,815/month for years 1-5
  • 30-year fixed at 6.8%: ~$2,005/month for 30 years
  • Savings years 1-5: ~$190/month = $11,400 total

That's attractive. But after year 5, if rates have risen to 8.5%, your ARM could adjust to 8.5%+, making your payment ~$2,350/month—$345 more than the fixed rate. You just lost all your savings and now pay more.

When ARMs make sense

  • You plan to sell or refinance within the fixed period: If you're buying a starter home and plan to upgrade in 5 years, a 5/1 ARM locks in savings for your tenure.
  • Rates are falling: In a declining rate environment and you're near the end of the fixed period, ARMs work well.
  • You have strong income growth expected: If you're a resident doctor or sales professional expecting significant income increases, an ARM's early savings help your budget now, and higher income later covers higher payments.
  • You're highly comfortable with risk:** ARMs require financial discipline and risk tolerance. If you're okay with potential swings, they can save money.

When ARMs are risky

  • You plan to stay 10+ years:** The longer you own the home, the more likely you'll face rate adjustments. ARMs typically make sense for shorter time horizons.
  • Your budget is tight: If you're already stretching to afford the starting payment, a rate increase could make the loan unaffordable.
  • Rates are historically low: If the current fixed rate is already low, there's more room for rates to rise than fall. ARMs are less attractive.
  • You have uncertain income:** If your job or income is unstable, payment uncertainty from an ARM is a liability.
  • You're a first-time buyer:** Experience helps with ARMs. Beginners typically benefit from simplicity and certainty of fixed rates.

Current environment (June 2026)

With rates around 6.8% fixed and ARMs starting around 6.2%, the savings are meaningful but not huge. Typical rate caps allow adjustments of 1-2% every few years, capped at 2-3% total over the loan term. Given economic uncertainty, the risk/reward of an ARM is less attractive today than in a clearly falling rate environment.

The math: break-even analysis

To decide, calculate:

  • Savings period: How much do you save monthly with the ARM? $190/month = $11,400 over 5 years.
  • How much can rates rise? If your ARM caps at 8.5%, what would your payment be? ~$2,350/month.
  • How long until you're ahead/behind? If rates jump to 8.5% in year 6, your payment shoots up $345. After 33 months (~2.75 years), you've erased all 5 years of savings.

If you're confident you'll sell or refinance within that time frame, the ARM wins. If not, the fixed rate's peace of mind is worth it.

Expert advice

Most financial advisors recommend fixed-rate mortgages for most borrowers, especially first-timers:

  • Simplicity and predictability reduce stress
  • You can refinance if rates drop, but you can't refinance away from rising rates on an ARM
  • The savings from an ARM (0.3-0.7%) don't justify the risk for most people

Bottom line

If you're buying your primary home and plan to stay 7+ years, a 30-year fixed-rate mortgage is your safest bet. If you're a sophisticated borrower with a clear exit timeline and can handle payment uncertainty, a 5/1 or 7/1 ARM can save money. But for most people? Lock in your rate, enjoy the predictability, and sleep easy.
