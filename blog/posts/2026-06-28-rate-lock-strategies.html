Rate lock strategies: timing your mortgage rate lock

June 28, 2026 · 6 min read

Locking your mortgage rate too early means you might miss better terms. Wait too long and you risk rates climbing. Mastering rate lock timing—and understanding lock options—can save or cost you significant money. Here's how to do it strategically.

What is a rate lock?

When you lock your rate, your lender commits to honoring that rate for a set period (usually 30-45 days, sometimes longer). Rates can change daily—sometimes hourly—so a lock keeps your rate stable while your loan processes.

  • Typical lock period: 30, 45, or 60 days
  • Lock cost: Usually free, though extended locks may have small fees
  • Lock commitment: Binding (both sides committed)
  • Early closing: If you close before your lock expires, that's fine. You get the lower rate.

Lock vs. float: the fundamental choice

Lock: Rate is guaranteed, but you can't benefit if rates drop.

Float: You stay flexible and can lock whenever you want, but you're exposed to rate increases.

Your choice depends on market conditions, your timeline, and your risk tolerance.

Strategic locking timeline

The ideal window: Lock when you're 30-60 days away from closing. This balances protection and flexibility.

If buying a resale home: Lock after you're under contract and have a firm closing date. You know your timeline, so lock appropriately.

If buying new construction: Lock closer to closing (45 days ideally) since your closing date is often uncertain.

If refinancing: Lock immediately after application, since you control the timing.

When to lock (market timing)

Lock immediately if:

  • Rates have dropped 0.5%+ from recent highs (likely to stabilize or bounce)
  • You're comfortable with current rates and don't want to risk an increase
  • You're closing within 30 days
  • Federal Reserve is signaling future rate hikes

Consider floating if:

  • Rates are at 10-year highs (likely room to drop)
  • You're 60+ days from closing (gives time for rates to move)
  • Central bank is signaling rate cuts
  • Economic data suggests weakness ahead (usually precedes rate drops)

Extended locks: when they make sense

Standard 30-45 day locks are often free. Extended locks (60-90 days) usually cost 0.125%-0.25% in higher rates. When are they worth it?

  • Long inspection period: If you expect a lengthy inspection/appraisal
  • Uncertain closing date: New construction or multiple contingencies
  • Rates likely to rise: During tightening cycles
  • Job change pending: Employment verification delays

Relock options

Some lenders offer relock protection—if rates drop after you lock, you can relock once at the lower rate. This is valuable and worth asking about. Usually it's free or a small cost.

  • Ask explicitly: "Do you offer relock options?"
  • Get it in writing
  • Understand any limitations (usually one relock per loan)

Float-down options

A float-down means you lock a higher rate upfront, then if rates drop, you can float down to lower rates (usually within a specific period like 7 days before closing).

  • Typical cost: 0.25%-0.5% higher rate
  • Provides insurance against rate increases while keeping upside
  • Good during very volatile markets

Lock expiration: what happens?

If your lock expires before you close:

  • You don't automatically get a new rate (rates may have moved)
  • You can request a new lock at current market rates
  • You can attempt to renegotiate with your lender
  • Most lenders will give you a new lock at market rates for your inconvenience

The psychology of locking

Many borrowers obsess over the exact moment to lock. Reality: timing the market perfectly is impossible. The difference between locking on the best vs. worst day is usually 0.125% or less—call it $20/month on a $300,000 loan. Don't let perfect be the enemy of good.

A reasonable strategy: Lock when rates are acceptable to you and your closing timeline is firm. Then move forward. Chasing lowest rates costs stress and often doesn't save money.

Locking in June 2026

Current market conditions (early summer, Fed signals clarity) favor locking when rates are stable. If you're closing in July or August, a 45-day lock starting now provides good protection. Ask your lender about relock options as downside protection.
