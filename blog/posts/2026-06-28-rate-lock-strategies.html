Rate lock strategies: timing your mortgage rate lock

June 28, 2026 · 5 min read

Once you get a mortgage rate quote, you face an immediate decision: lock it in now, or float and hope rates drop? This is one of the trickiest decisions in the home-buying process. Let's walk through the strategy.

What is a rate lock?

A rate lock is a guarantee that your interest rate won't change between now and closing (typically 30-60 days later). Once you lock, your rate is protected—even if market rates jump. In exchange, you lose the upside if rates drop.

  • Lock period: Typically 30, 45, or 60 days (sometimes longer)
  • What's protected: Your interest rate and discount points
  • What's not protected: ARMs, which can still adjust after lock expiration
  • Cost: Usually free, though some lenders charge small fees for longer locks (60+ days)

Floating: the alternative

Floating means your rate isn't locked. If market rates drop, your rate drops with them (good for you). If rates rise, you're stuck paying more (bad for you). Floating is a gamble.

  • When it works: Rates drop, you get a better deal
  • When it doesn't: Rates rise, and now you're paying above-market rates
  • Risk: Unlimited—rates could rise 0.5% or more
  • Potential reward: 0.1-0.25% reduction if rates drop

The psychological aspect

Most people float hoping rates drop, then panic as rates inch up and lock in a worse rate than the original quote. This is called "rate regret." The data shows that over time, people who float pay more on average than those who lock immediately. Why? Because the average market movement is flat to up, and the stress causes poor decision-making.

When to lock immediately

  • You're happy with your rate: If 6.8% feels good to you, lock it. Perfection is the enemy of good.
  • Rates are trending up: If economic data is getting stronger or the Fed is signaling tightening, lock now.
  • You're close to your rate-lock deadline: If your lock expires in 3 days and you still need 15 days to close, lock an extension now.
  • You have a short close timeline: The shorter your window, the more important certainty is. Lock if you close in under 21 days.
  • You're 10% down or less: Lower down payments = bigger mortgage = a 0.5% rate change costs you $150/month. Lock the certainty.

When to float (cautiously)

  • You have a long rate-lock period (60+ days): More time increases the chance rates drop.
  • Rates are historically high and potential policy changes are supportive of rates dropping: E.g., if the Fed is expected to cut rates, floating makes sense.
  • You have flexibility to delay closing if needed: If rates tick up but are still reasonable, you can accept it.
  • Market volatility is low: Calm markets are safer to float. Volatile markets punish floaters.

The risk/reward math

Let's say you're floating on a 6.8% rate, and you're considering locking. Scenario analysis:

Scenario A: Rates drop to 6.5% (best case for floating)

  • Your float wins: Save ~$65/month on a $300,000 loan ($23,400 over 30 years)

Scenario B: Rates stay at 6.8% (neutral)

  • Lock and float are equal

Scenario C: Rates rise to 7.3% (worst case for floating)

  • Your float loses: Pay ~$130/month more on the same loan ($46,800 over 30 years)

Notice the asymmetry? The downside (rates up 0.5%) is twice as costly as the upside (rates down 0.3%). Mathematically, floating only makes sense if you think there's more than a 67% chance rates will drop.

Professional guidance: what do advisors say?

Most loan officers and financial advisors recommend locking for most buyers. Why? Because:

  • Certainty has value—knowing your payment eliminates stress
  • The historical bet has been that rates stay flat or rise
  • Most floaters end up locking anyway (usually at a worse rate) when they get nervous
  • You can always refinance later if rates drop significantly

The hybrid approach: rate lock with a float-down option

Some lenders offer "float-down" features where you can lower your rate once if rates drop before closing. You pay a small fee (0.125-0.25%) for this option. It's a middle ground: you have certainty, plus a one-time backstop if rates drop.

Final advice

Lock your rate when you're comfortable with it. Chasing perfect rates leads to regret and delays. A 6.8% rate locked today beats a 6.5% rate locked later that you lose sleep over. Move forward with confidence, and if rates drop meaningfully (0.5%+), you can refinance in 6-12 months.
