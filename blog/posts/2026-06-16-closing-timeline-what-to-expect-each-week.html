Closing timeline: what to expect each week

June 16, 2026 • Michael Miller

The period between offer acceptance and closing day is filled with activity—appraisals, inspections, paperwork, and waiting. Knowing what happens each week helps you stay ahead of deadlines and avoid delays that could cost you your dream home.

The Standard Timeline: 30-45 Days

Most conventional and FHA mortgages close in 30-45 days from accepted offer. Cash purchases can close in as little as 7-14 days. Here's what typically happens week by week.

Week 1: Offer Accepted—The Clock Starts

  • Day 1-2: Sign purchase agreement, submit earnest money deposit (typically 1-3% of purchase price).
  • Day 1-3: Formally apply for mortgage with your lender. Submit pay stubs, tax returns, bank statements, ID.
  • Day 3-5: Lender orders title search and begins processing your application.
  • Day 5-7: Schedule home inspection (due diligence period begins).

Your action: Respond to lender document requests within 24 hours. Delays here cascade through the entire process.

Week 2: Inspections and Appraisal

  • Day 7-10: Home inspection completed. Review report for major issues.
  • Day 8-12: If inspection reveals problems, negotiate repairs or price reduction with seller.
  • Day 10-14: Lender orders appraisal. Appraiser visits property.
  • Day 10-14: Lender issues Loan Estimate (LE) if not already provided.

Potential delays: Low appraisal (requires renegotiation), inspection issues requiring repair negotiations, or specialty inspections (radon, septic, termite) taking extra time.

Week 3: Underwriting Begins

  • Day 14-18: Appraisal report received. Lender reviews for value and condition issues.
  • Day 15-21: File enters underwriting. Underwriter reviews all documentation.
  • Day 18-21: Underwriter issues conditional approval or requests additional documentation.

Common conditions: Letter of explanation for large deposits, updated pay stubs, verification of employment (VOE), proof of insurance.

Your action: Clear conditions immediately. Every day of delay pushes your closing date.

Week 4: Clearing Conditions

  • Day 21-25: Submit all requested documentation. Underwriter re-reviews file.
  • Day 22-26: Title company resolves any title issues (liens, judgments, boundary disputes).
  • Day 25-28: Clear to close (CTC) issued—this means underwriting is complete.

Critical milestone: Clear to close means you're approved and the lender is ready to fund. Without CTC, you can't close on schedule.

Week 5: Final Steps and Closing

  • Day 28-30: Receive Closing Disclosure (CD) at least 3 business days before closing (required by law).
  • Day 29-32: Review CD carefully—compare to Loan Estimate for changes.
  • Day 30-33: Final walkthrough of property (typically 24-48 hours before closing).
  • Closing day: Sign documents (plan 1-2 hours), wire funds or bring cashier's check for closing costs.

After closing: Lender funds the loan, title company records the deed, you receive keys.

What Can Delay Closing?

Buyer-Related Delays:

  • Slow document submission (most common cause of delays)
  • Job change or income disruption during process
  • Large purchases or new credit accounts opened
  • Bank statements with unexplained deposits
  • Missing or incomplete documentation

Property-Related Delays:

  • Low appraisal requiring renegotiation
  • Appraisal condition requirements (repairs needed)
  • Title issues (liens, ownership disputes, probate)
  • Survey problems (encroachments, easements)
  • HOA document delays

Lender-Related Delays:

  • Backlog during busy seasons
  • Underwriter requests for additional conditions
  • Third-party verification delays (employment, deposits)

How to Avoid Delays

  • Get pre-approved before making offers: Full pre-approval, not just pre-qualification.
  • Respond to requests same-day: Treat lender emails like emergencies.
  • Don't make major financial changes: No new cars, no furniture purchases, no job changes.
  • Keep documents organized: Have tax returns, pay stubs, and bank statements ready.
  • Communicate proactively: Ask your loan officer for status updates weekly.

What If You Need to Extend?

If delays push past your scheduled closing date:

  • Notify seller immediately—don't wait until the deadline.
  • Request extension in writing (typically 7-14 days).
  • Seller may require compensation (per diem charges) or amendment to contract.
  • Rate lock extensions from your lender may cost money if your lock expires.

The Bottom Line

Closing on a home is a coordinated effort involving you, your lender, title company, real estate agents, inspectors, appraisers, and the seller. Your job is to be responsive, organized, and patient. Respond to every request quickly, avoid financial changes, and trust the process. In 30-45 days, you'll have the keys to your new home.
