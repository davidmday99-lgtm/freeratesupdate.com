ARM vs fixed-rate mortgages: which is right for you

Sunday, May 17, 2026 • Michael Miller

One of the biggest decisions in mortgage shopping isn't about which lender to choose—it's about which type of loan to get. Should you lock in a fixed rate for 30 years, or take a chance on an adjustable-rate mortgage (ARM)? The answer depends on your situation, your risk tolerance, and your timeline.

Fixed-rate mortgages: predictability and peace of mind

With a fixed-rate mortgage, your interest rate stays the same for the entire life of the loan—typically 15, 20, or 30 years. Your monthly principal and interest payment never changes. If you get a 6.5% rate today, you'll pay 6.5% in year 5, year 15, and year 30.

This is the choice most borrowers make, and for good reason. Stability matters. You can budget predictably. You're protected if rates spike. And historically, when rates are rising or high, fixed rates make even more sense because you've locked in protection against further increases.

Today's rate environment is a good example: if fixed rates are around 6.0% to 6.5%, most buyers benefit from the certainty. You eliminate the risk that rates could rise to 7% or 8% mid-loan.

Adjustable-rate mortgages: lower initial rates, higher risk

An ARM typically starts with a lower interest rate than a fixed loan—often 0.5% to 1.0% lower. That lower "teaser" rate is fixed for a period (commonly 3, 5, 7, or 10 years), then resets annually or semi-annually based on market conditions.

Example: A 5/1 ARM might offer 5.5% for the first 5 years, then reset based on current rates. If rates have risen to 7.5%, your payment jumps. If they've fallen to 4.5%, you benefit from the lower rate.

ARMs typically include rate caps—limits on how much the rate can increase per adjustment and over the life of the loan. For example, a 2/6 cap means your rate can't jump more than 2% per adjustment or 6% total over the loan's lifetime. But even with caps, your payment can increase significantly.

When an ARM makes sense

  • Short-term ownership: If you plan to sell or refinance within 5-7 years, an ARM's lower initial rate saves you money without the risk of rate adjustments hitting you hard.
  • Income growth: If your income is rising and you expect higher payments to be manageable in a few years, an ARM's lower early payments can help you qualify for the home you want.
  • Falling rate expectations: If you believe rates will drop during the loan term, an ARM positions you to benefit. (This is speculative, though—rates are unpredictable.)
  • Buying leverage: In competitive markets, an ARM might lower your monthly payment enough to qualify for a bigger purchase price than a fixed loan would allow.

When a fixed-rate is the smarter choice

  • Long-term ownership: If you plan to stay 10+ years, rate adjustments pose real risk. Fixed-rate stability is worth the slightly higher starting rate.
  • Rising or high-rate environment: When rates are 6.0% or higher, locking in protection against further increases is usually smart. The discount an ARM offers often doesn't justify the uncertainty.
  • Tight budget: If higher payments would strain your finances, don't bet on rates staying low. Fixed-rate gives you control and predictability.
  • Peace of mind: If market volatility stresses you, fixed-rate eliminates that variable. That peace of mind has real value.

The numbers: when does an ARM actually save you money?

Let's say you're buying a $350,000 home with 20% down ($280,000 loan). A 30-year fixed at 6.3% costs about $1,720/month in principal and interest. A 5/1 ARM at 5.8% costs about $1,640/month in year one—saving you $80/month, or about $4,800 over five years.

But when that ARM adjusts in year 6, if rates have risen to 7.3%, your new rate might be capped at 7.3% (depending on caps), raising your payment to around $1,860/month—$140 more than the original fixed payment. Over the remaining 25 years, that difference compounds significantly.

The ARM saves money only if you sell before year 6, or if rates remain stable or fall. Most people who take ARMs don't hit their happy scenario—they end up paying more overall.

The smart approach: compare your options

Get rate quotes for both fixed and ARM options. Look beyond just the payment difference. Calculate what happens at adjustment time using worst-case scenarios (rate caps at their maximum). Ask your lender for payment examples at reset dates. Then honestly assess: Will I still afford this if my payment jumps by $200, $300, or more per month?

For most homebuyers, especially those buying their first home or planning to stay long-term, a fixed-rate mortgage remains the safest, most predictable choice. ARMs have their place, but they're best suited for sophisticated borrowers with specific timelines and the financial cushion to absorb payment shocks.
