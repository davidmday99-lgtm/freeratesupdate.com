Investment property financing: what lenders require

June 17, 2026 • Michael Miller

Financing an investment property isn't like buying your primary residence. Lenders view rental properties as higher risk, which means stricter requirements, larger down payments, and higher interest rates. Here's what you need to know before you buy that rental.

Why Investment Properties Are Different

Lenders know that when financial stress hits, borrowers prioritize their home over their investments. Default rates on investment properties are historically higher than primary residences. To compensate for this risk, lenders impose tougher standards.

Down Payment Requirements

Forget 3% down—investment properties require significant skin in the game:

  • Single-family investment: 15-25% minimum down payment
  • 2-4 unit investment: 20-25% minimum down payment
  • Portfolio/5+ units: 25-30% typical, commercial lending standards apply

The exact requirement depends on your credit score, the property type, and the lender. Higher down payments often unlock better rates.

Interest Rate Premium

Expect to pay 0.5% to 0.875% more than primary residence rates:

  • If today's primary residence rate is 6.5%, investment property rate is approximately 7.0-7.375%.
  • Second homes (vacation properties) fall between primary and investment rates.
  • Rate adjustments also apply for multi-unit properties, lower credit scores, and cash-out refinances.

Credit Score Requirements

Investment property loans have higher credit thresholds:

  • Conventional investment loans: 620 minimum, but 680+ gets better pricing
  • Best rates: 740+ credit score
  • Each tier below 740: Adds pricing adjustments that increase your rate

Reserve Requirements

Lenders require you to have cash reserves after closing—money in savings that could cover mortgage payments if rental income stops:

  • Primary residence: 0-2 months reserves typical
  • Investment property: 6-12 months reserves required
  • Multiple investment properties: Reserves required for EACH property

Example: If your investment property payment is $2,000/month, you need $12,000-$24,000 in verified liquid assets after closing.

Debt-to-Income Ratio (DTI)

Investment loans have stricter DTI limits:

  • Maximum DTI: Usually 43-45% (lower than some primary residence programs)
  • Rental income offset: Lenders typically count 75% of expected rental income to offset the new mortgage payment
  • Existing rental income: Must be documented with tax returns showing 2+ years of landlord experience

How Rental Income Is Counted

If you're buying a property with existing tenants or documented market rents:

  • Appraiser provides comparable rent analysis or uses existing lease
  • Lender applies 75% factor (25% vacancy/expense allowance)
  • Net rental income offsets the property's mortgage payment in DTI calculation

Example: Property rents for $2,000/month. Lender counts $1,500 (75%) as income offset against $1,800 mortgage payment = only $300 added to your DTI.

Loan Types for Investment Properties

1. Conventional Investment Loans

  • Most common option for 1-4 unit investment properties
  • Backed by Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac guidelines
  • Best rates for well-qualified borrowers
  • Limit: 10 financed properties total (including primary residence)

2. DSCR Loans (Debt Service Coverage Ratio)

  • Qualify based on property cash flow, not personal income
  • Great for self-employed investors or those with complex tax returns
  • Higher rates than conventional (typically 1-2% higher)
  • Minimum DSCR of 1.0-1.25 (rental income must cover 100-125% of mortgage payment)

3. Portfolio Loans

  • Held by lender (not sold to Fannie/Freddie)
  • More flexible underwriting
  • Good for investors exceeding conventional property limits
  • Often adjustable rate or shorter fixed periods

4. Commercial Loans (5+ Units)

  • Properties with 5+ units are commercial, not residential
  • Different underwriting focused on property performance
  • Shorter loan terms (5-10 year terms with 25-30 year amortization)
  • Balloon payments common

House Hacking: The Workaround

Want investment property benefits with primary residence financing? House hacking is the answer:

  • Buy a 2-4 unit property
  • Live in one unit as your primary residence
  • Qualify with primary residence requirements (3.5% FHA, 5% conventional)
  • Rent other units to offset your mortgage
  • After 1 year, you can move out and keep the property as a rental

Documentation Required

Prepare to provide:

  • 2 years tax returns (personal and business if applicable)
  • 2 years W-2s or 1099s
  • 2 months bank statements showing reserves
  • Rent roll for existing investment properties
  • Lease agreements for properties you already own
  • Property insurance quotes
  • Entity documents if purchasing through LLC (often not allowed for residential loans)

LLC Ownership: The Catch

Many investors want to hold properties in LLCs for liability protection. The catch:

  • Most residential mortgage programs require personal name on title
  • You can transfer to LLC after closing, but this may trigger due-on-sale clause
  • Some lenders allow LLC ownership with personal guarantee
  • Commercial loans readily allow LLC ownership

Strategy: Build Your Portfolio

  • Start with house hacking: Lower barriers, learn landlording while living there.
  • Save aggressively for 20-25% down: Investment properties reward patient savers.
  • Build credit above 740: Every point matters for investment property pricing.
  • Document rental income properly: Report all rental income on taxes to build qualifying history.
  • Work with investment-focused lenders: Not all lenders understand investor needs.

The Bottom Line

Investment property financing is harder than primary residence financing, but millions of investors make it work. Plan for larger down payments, higher rates, and strict documentation requirements. The reward: rental income, appreciation, and building long-term wealth through real estate.
