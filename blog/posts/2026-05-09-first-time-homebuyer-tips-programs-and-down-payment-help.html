First-time homebuyer tips, programs, and down payment help

May 9, 2026 • Michael Miller

Buying your first home is one of the biggest decisions you'll make. The good news? You don't have to do it alone, and you don't need 20% down. Here's what every first-time buyer should know.

Down payment myths—and the truth

The biggest myth is that you need to save 20% down. In reality, many buyers put down 3% to 5%—sometimes even less. FHA loans allow down payments as low as 3.5%, and VA loans don't require a down payment at all for eligible veterans. Conventional loans increasingly work with 5% down. Lower down payments mean you can buy sooner, build equity faster, and start building wealth in your own home.

Yes, lower down payments typically mean mortgage insurance (PMI for conventional, MIP for FHA). But that's temporary and often worth the trade-off if it gets you into a home now versus waiting years to save more cash.

Programs designed to help you

  • FHA loans: Designed specifically for first-time buyers. Accept credit scores as low as 580, allow 3.5% down, and have flexible underwriting. Your payment will include mortgage insurance, but the rates and accessibility make them worth comparing.
  • VA loans: If you served, this is one of the best deals available. Zero down, no PMI, no prepayment penalties. Period.
  • USDA loans: Buying outside a city? USDA loans offer 100% financing in rural and suburban areas for income-eligible buyers.
  • State and local programs: Many states and cities offer down payment assistance, closing cost grants, or favorable terms for first-time buyers. Check your local housing authority or your state's mortgage finance agency.
  • Employer programs: Some employers offer first-time homebuyer grants or matched savings programs. Ask HR—you might be surprised.

What lenders look at beyond credit

Your credit score matters, but it's not everything. Lenders check your debt-to-income ratio (typically under 43%), employment history, and cash reserves. If your credit is below 620, FHA is often your best path. If your score is above 640, conventional loans become competitive. Getting pre-approved shows sellers you're serious and reveals exactly what you can afford before you start shopping.

The real costs you need to know

Beyond the down payment, budget for closing costs (1% to 3% of the purchase price), inspections, appraisals, homeowners insurance, and property taxes. Some programs let the seller cover part of your closing costs—negotiate this during offers. Also factor in HOA fees if applicable and reserve 1% of your home's value annually for maintenance.

Your next step

Start with getting pre-approved. It costs nothing, takes 20 minutes, and shows you exactly what you qualify for. Compare your options across FHA, VA, USDA, and conventional loans. The "best" loan isn't about the lowest rate—it's about what works for your timeline, your down payment, and your financial goals.
