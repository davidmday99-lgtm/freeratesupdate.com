Mortgage preapproval vs prequalification: what's the difference

June 18, 2026 • Michael Miller

When you start shopping for a home, lenders offer prequalification and preapproval. Many buyers use these terms interchangeably—but they're not the same. One is a rough estimate; the other is a verified commitment. Knowing the difference helps you compete effectively in today's market.

Prequalification: The Quick Estimate

Prequalification is an informal assessment based on information you provide verbally or through a simple form. No documentation is verified.

What's Involved:

  • You state your income, debts, assets, and employment
  • Lender may or may not pull your credit (soft pull if they do)
  • Takes 10-30 minutes
  • You receive a letter stating approximate loan amount you "may" qualify for

What It Means:

  • No guarantee of approval
  • Based entirely on self-reported information
  • Lender hasn't verified income, assets, or employment
  • The number is an estimate, not a commitment

Preapproval: The Real Deal

Preapproval is a verified commitment based on documented income, assets, credit, and employment. The lender has done real underwriting work.

What's Involved:

  • Full mortgage application submitted
  • Credit report pulled (hard inquiry)
  • Income verified through pay stubs, tax returns, W-2s
  • Assets verified through bank statements
  • Employment verified directly with employer
  • Debt obligations confirmed through credit report
  • Underwriter reviews file and issues conditional approval

What It Means:

  • Lender has committed to lending you a specific amount
  • Approval is conditional only on property (appraisal, title, inspection)
  • Your financial qualification is essentially complete
  • Much stronger position when making offers

Side-by-Side Comparison

  • Time to complete: Prequalification = minutes | Preapproval = 1-3 days
  • Documentation required: Prequalification = none | Preapproval = full documentation
  • Credit check: Prequalification = optional soft pull | Preapproval = hard pull
  • Verification: Prequalification = self-reported | Preapproval = verified
  • Commitment level: Prequalification = estimate | Preapproval = conditional commitment
  • Seller confidence: Prequalification = low | Preapproval = high

Why Sellers Care

Experienced sellers and listing agents know the difference:

  • Prequalification letter: "This buyer thinks they can afford the house, but we haven't checked."
  • Preapproval letter: "This buyer is verified and ready to close—financing is unlikely to fall through."

In competitive markets with multiple offers, preapproved buyers often win over prequalified buyers—even with slightly lower offers. Certainty has value.

The Underwritten Preapproval: Gold Standard

Some lenders offer "underwritten preapproval" or "verified preapproval"—an even stronger commitment:

  • Full underwriting completed before you find a property
  • Only the property itself needs approval (appraisal, title)
  • Can close faster than standard preapproval
  • Maximum confidence for sellers

If available, this is worth the extra effort in hot markets.

When Prequalification Makes Sense

Prequalification isn't useless—it serves a purpose:

  • Very early in your home search: Get a rough idea of your budget before committing to full documentation
  • Testing the waters: See if homeownership is realistic before pulling your credit
  • Comparing lender estimates: Quick way to see rough rates from multiple lenders

Just don't confuse it with being ready to make competitive offers.

The Preapproval Process: What to Expect

Step 1: Gather Documents

  • 2 years of tax returns (all pages, all schedules)
  • 2 years of W-2s or 1099s
  • 30 days of pay stubs
  • 2 months of bank statements (all pages)
  • Government-issued ID
  • Social Security number

Step 2: Submit Application

  • Complete full mortgage application (1003 form)
  • Authorize credit pull
  • Upload all documentation

Step 3: Verification and Review

  • Lender verifies employment directly
  • Underwriter reviews income, assets, credit
  • Lender calculates maximum loan amount and estimated terms

Step 4: Receive Preapproval Letter

  • Letter states maximum purchase price or loan amount
  • Valid for 60-90 days typically
  • Lender can customize letter for specific property amounts

Does Preapproval Hurt Your Credit?

Yes, preapproval requires a hard credit inquiry, which can temporarily lower your score by 5-10 points. However:

  • Multiple mortgage inquiries within 14-45 days (varies by scoring model) count as one inquiry
  • You can shop multiple lenders within this window without additional impact
  • The impact fades quickly and is far outweighed by the benefit of being preapproved

How Long Does Preapproval Last?

Preapproval letters typically expire in 60-90 days. After that:

  • Updated documentation may be required (new pay stubs, bank statements)
  • Credit may be re-pulled
  • Employment re-verified
  • Any changes in your financial situation must be disclosed

Don't Let Preapproval Expire Before Closing

Even after your offer is accepted, your preapproval needs to remain valid. Keep documentation updated and avoid major financial changes until you close.

The Bottom Line

In serious home searches, preapproval is essential. It tells sellers you're qualified and ready. Prequalification is fine for early exploration, but don't make offers without preapproval—you'll lose competitive situations and waste everyone's time. Get preapproved before you start viewing homes, and you'll shop with confidence and compete effectively.
