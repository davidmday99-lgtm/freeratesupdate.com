Mortgage insurance explained: PMI vs MIP

June 15, 2026 • Michael Miller

Mortgage insurance confuses many buyers. It protects the lender, not you—yet you pay for it. Understanding the difference between PMI (Private Mortgage Insurance) and MIP (Mortgage Insurance Premium) helps you choose the right loan and plan for when you can stop paying.

What Is Mortgage Insurance?

Mortgage insurance is a policy that protects the lender if you default on your loan. It exists because borrowers with less than 20% down are considered higher risk. The insurance covers the lender's losses if they have to foreclose.

  • PMI applies to conventional loans (backed by Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac)
  • MIP applies to FHA loans (insured by the Federal Housing Administration)
  • Both add to your monthly payment, but the costs and removal rules differ significantly

PMI: Private Mortgage Insurance (Conventional Loans)

PMI is required on conventional loans when your down payment is less than 20%.

How PMI Costs Are Calculated:

  • Typically 0.5% to 1.5% of your loan amount annually
  • Factors: Credit score, down payment size, loan-to-value ratio, loan type
  • Higher credit scores = lower PMI rates
  • Example: $300,000 loan at 0.8% PMI = $2,400/year = $200/month

How to Remove PMI:

  • Automatic removal: Lenders must cancel PMI when your loan balance reaches 78% of the original value.
  • Request removal: You can request cancellation at 80% LTV with a good payment history.
  • Reappraisal option: If your home has appreciated significantly, get a new appraisal to prove 20%+ equity.
  • Refinance: Refinance into a new loan without PMI if you have 20%+ equity.

MIP: Mortgage Insurance Premium (FHA Loans)

FHA loans require MIP regardless of your down payment. MIP has two components:

1. Upfront MIP (UFMIP):

  • 1.75% of the loan amount, paid at closing
  • Can be rolled into your loan balance (most borrowers do this)
  • Example: $300,000 loan = $5,250 UFMIP

2. Annual MIP:

  • 0.55% annually for most FHA loans (varies by loan term and LTV)
  • Paid monthly as part of your mortgage payment
  • Example: $300,000 loan at 0.55% = $1,650/year = $137.50/month

FHA MIP Removal Rules (The Catch):

  • Down payment 10%+: MIP can be removed after 11 years.
  • Down payment less than 10%: MIP lasts for the life of the loan.
  • Only way out: Refinance into a conventional loan when you have 20%+ equity.

PMI vs MIP: Side-by-Side Comparison

  • Applies to: PMI = Conventional | MIP = FHA
  • Upfront cost: PMI = None typically | MIP = 1.75%
  • Annual cost: PMI = 0.5-1.5% | MIP = 0.55%
  • Removal: PMI = At 80% LTV | MIP = After 11 years or never
  • Credit sensitivity: PMI = Very sensitive | MIP = Fixed rates

Which Is Better: PMI or MIP?

It depends on your situation:

  • Strong credit (700+): Conventional with PMI is usually cheaper. PMI rates reward good credit, and you can cancel it sooner.
  • Lower credit (below 680): FHA with MIP may be cheaper. FHA doesn't penalize credit as harshly, and the fixed MIP rate might beat your PMI quote.
  • Short-term ownership: If you'll sell in 5-7 years, the type matters less—run the numbers for your specific timeline.
  • Long-term ownership: Conventional with PMI wins long-term because you can eliminate it; FHA MIP can last forever.

Strategies to Minimize Mortgage Insurance Costs

1. Increase Your Down Payment

  • Even moving from 5% to 10% down can significantly reduce PMI costs.
  • For FHA, putting 10%+ down unlocks the 11-year MIP removal rule.

2. Improve Your Credit Before Applying

  • PMI rates drop substantially as credit scores rise.
  • A 740+ score can cut your PMI in half compared to a 680 score.

3. Lender-Paid PMI (LPMI)

  • Some lenders offer to pay PMI in exchange for a higher interest rate.
  • Good if you'll keep the loan short-term; bad for long-term ownership.
  • Warning: LPMI can't be removed—you'd have to refinance.

4. Piggyback Loans (80-10-10)

  • First mortgage at 80% LTV, second mortgage at 10%, 10% down payment.
  • Avoids PMI entirely, but second mortgage has higher interest rate.
  • Run the math carefully—sometimes PMI is cheaper.

When to Refinance Out of MIP

If you have an FHA loan with lifetime MIP, monitor your equity. Once you reach 20%, compare refinance costs against MIP savings:

  • Calculate remaining MIP payments over your expected ownership period.
  • Compare to refinance closing costs plus any rate change impact.
  • If MIP savings exceed refinance costs within 2-3 years, refinancing makes sense.

The Bottom Line

Mortgage insurance is the cost of buying with less than 20% down—and for most buyers, it's worth paying to get into a home sooner. Just understand what you're signing up for: PMI offers a clear exit path at 80% equity, while FHA MIP can be permanent. Factor this into your loan decision, and plan your path to eliminate the cost as quickly as your situation allows.
