Mortgage insurance explained: PMI, FHA MIP, VA funding fees

June 30, 2026 · 6 min read

One of the biggest surprises for first-time home buyers is mortgage insurance—an extra cost that can add $150-300+ to your monthly payment. Let's break down what these fees are, why they exist, and how to minimize or eliminate them.

Why mortgage insurance exists

When you put down less than 20%, the lender takes extra risk. You have less skin in the game, which historically means higher default rates. Mortgage insurance protects the lender (not you) if you stop paying. It's not optional below 20% down on conventional loans.

Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI)

PMI is the insurance required on conventional mortgages when your down payment is below 20%.

  • Who pays: You do, monthly as part of your mortgage payment
  • Cost: Typically 0.3-1.5% of the loan amount annually, paid monthly. On a $300,000 loan, that's $75-375/month
  • Why it varies: Credit score, loan-to-value ratio (LTV), and loan type all affect your PMI rate
  • Duration: Required until you hit 20% equity (usually through payments + home appreciation)
  • Cancellation: You can request PMI removal once you reach 20% equity. Your lender must drop it at 22% equity automatically

Example: $300,000 home, $30,000 down (10%), $270,000 loan at 6.8% with 0.6% PMI

  • Base mortgage payment: ~$1,804
  • PMI: ~$135/month
  • Total with PMI: ~$1,939/month

FHA Mortgage Insurance Premium (MIP)

FHA loans require mortgage insurance regardless of down payment, but it's structured differently than PMI.

  • Two components:
    • Upfront MIP: 1.75% of loan amount, charged at closing (often rolled into your loan)
    • Annual MIP: 0.35-0.80% of loan amount annually, paid monthly
  • Duration: If down payment was less than 10%, MIP lasts the entire life of the loan. If 10%+ down, MIP ends after 11 years
  • Cost on $300k home with 3.5% down ($10,500):
    • Upfront MIP: ~$5,250 (rolled into loan)
    • Annual MIP: ~$1,560-3,510 (~$130-293/month)

FHA insurance is more expensive overall, especially if you keep the loan long-term. However, FHA loans have slightly lower rates than conventional counterparts, which sometimes offsets the insurance cost.

VA Funding Fee

VA loans don't have mortgage insurance, but they have a funding fee (a one-time charge to support the VA loan program).

  • Cost: 1.25-3.3% of loan amount, depending on military service branch and down payment
  • Payment: Typically paid at closing or rolled into the loan
  • Waiver: Disabled veterans and some other groups are exempt
  • Example on $300k loan: ~$3,750-9,900 one-time fee (not monthly)

Even with a funding fee, VA loans are often cheaper long-term because there's no recurring mortgage insurance.

Comparing the three

On a $300,000 home with 5% down ($15,000):

Loan Type Monthly Insurance/Fee Upfront Cost Duration
Conventional + PMI ~$225-300 $0 Until 20% equity
FHA ~$165-280 ~$5,250 Life of loan* (*if <10% down)
VA (with funding fee) $0 ~$5,600 Never (one-time fee)

Over a 30-year loan, the conventional buyer with PMI pays ~$80,000-108,000 in total insurance. The FHA buyer pays ~$59,400-100,800 (upfront + monthly). The VA buyer pays ~$5,600 and nothing more. If you qualify for VA, it's the clear winner.

How to reduce or eliminate mortgage insurance

Strategy 1: Save for 20% down – The simplest way to avoid PMI altogether on a conventional loan is to put down 20%. This means saving more upfront but eliminating years of insurance payments.

Strategy 2: Piggyback loans (80/10/10) – Instead of one $300,000 loan at 85% LTV (with PMI), get an 80% first mortgage + 10% second mortgage + 10% down. This avoids PMI but adds a second payment. Only works if you have the credit for both.

Strategy 3: Pay down faster – Make extra principal payments early in the loan. The faster you hit 20% equity, the sooner you eliminate PMI.

Strategy 4: Refinance into FHA if your credit improves** – If you start with a conventional loan and your credit score jumps, refinancing into FHA might lower your overall cost (FHA has lower rates, even with insurance).

Strategy 5: Use a lender credit to buy down your rate – Some conventional lenders will credit you closing costs in exchange for a slightly higher rate and PMI. PMI is tax-deductible (in most cases), so the true cost is a bit lower.

Strategy 6: Choose VA if eligible** – If you're a veteran, don't hesitate. VA loans + no mortgage insurance = best long-term deal.

Is mortgage insurance always bad?

Not quite. While it adds cost, mortgage insurance enables you to buy sooner with less savings. If real estate prices are rising 5%/year and you can buy now instead of waiting 2 years to save 20% down, the mortgage insurance cost is often justified—you're capturing 10% appreciation instead of waiting.

The key is understanding the cost and having a plan to eliminate it (either by payment or refinancing).

Final thoughts

Mortgage insurance isn't the end of the world, but it's a real cost. Shop carefully: a conventional loan with PMI might have lower rates than an FHA loan; compare total monthly payments, not just insurance amounts. If you qualify for VA, use it. And start planning how you'll eliminate insurance as early as possible—either by saving extra for principal payments or refinancing when conditions allow.
