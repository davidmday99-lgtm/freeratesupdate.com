First-time homebuyer: mortgage mistakes to avoid

June 9, 2026 • Michael Miller

First-time buyers often make the same mistakes. Most are preventable if you know what to watch. Here are the biggest pitfalls and how to sidestep them:

Mistake #1: Not Getting Pre-Approved Before House Hunting

This is the most common and most costly mistake.

  • Pre-approval tells you your real budget. Without it, you might fall in love with a house you can't afford.
  • Sellers take you seriously with a pre-approval letter. Without it, your offer loses to other buyers instantly.
  • Pre-approval is free and takes 24–48 hours. There's no downside.

Mistake #2: Applying for New Credit Before Closing

After you get pre-approved, don't mess with your credit:

  • New car? New credit card? New furniture loan? Wait until after closing.
  • A hard inquiry can lower your score by 5–10 points. New debt raises your debt-to-income ratio.
  • Lenders pull your credit again at closing. If something changed, your loan could be denied.

Mistake #3: Changing Jobs or Employment Status

Job changes matter to lenders more than you'd think:

  • If you're on an hourly or commission-based job, lenders want to see 2 years of history. A jump to a new field raises red flags.
  • If you just changed jobs, document why and show that your income won't drop (or prove it'll increase).
  • Best practice: stay in your current job until 30 days after closing.

Mistake #4: Quitting Your Job or Going Self-Employed

This is a deal-killer, even if you make more:

  • Lenders want proof of stable income. Self-employment needs 2 years of tax returns to verify.
  • If you just started a business, you're usually denied. They'll underwrite you again in 2 years.
  • If you stayed at your job but were offered a better opportunity, negotiate to start after closing.

Mistake #5: Making Large Deposits Without Documentation

Your lender will ask where your down payment came from:

  • If you deposit $20,000 in cash without explanation, they'll ask for proof it's legitimate and not borrowed money.
  • Borrowed down payment money disqualifies you (lenders want equity, not leverage stacked higher).
  • If a family member is gifting funds, they need to provide a gift letter stating it's not a loan.

Mistake #6: Not Shopping Multiple Lenders

This mistake costs thousands:

  • Rates vary by 0.25–0.75% between lenders. On a $400,000 mortgage, that's $1,000–$3,000/year.
  • Get quotes from at least 3 lenders. Use a rate comparison sheet so you're comparing identical loan terms.
  • Don't just look at interest rate. Compare points, origination fees, and closing costs.

Mistake #7: Assuming You Need 20% Down

This myth keeps people renting longer than necessary:

  • FHA loans require only 3.5% down. Conventional loans work with 5–10% down.
  • Yes, you'll pay PMI (mortgage insurance) with less than 20% down, but homeownership equity beats rent.
  • PMI can be removed once you hit 20% equity (usually in 7–10 years).

Mistake #8: Ignoring the Loan Estimate

Your Loan Estimate is your roadmap:

  • Read it carefully. Compare estimates from multiple lenders.
  • Ask about every fee. Some are required (appraisal, credit report), others are negotiable (underwriting, processing).
  • Watch for "garbage fees"—lender junk fees that add no value. Shop around if you see them.

Mistake #9: Not Locking Your Rate (When You Should)

Rate locks are crucial:

  • If you can't close for 45+ days, lock your rate immediately. Rates can jump 0.5% in a week.
  • Look for a float-down option if rates drop during your lock period. It usually costs 0.125–0.25%.
  • Know when your lock expires. If appraisals are delayed and your lock is about to expire, get it extended (ask your lender early).

Mistake #10: Skipping the Final Walkthrough

This isn't a mortgage mistake, but it affects your whole deal:

  • 24 hours before closing, do a final walkthrough. Verify the house is in agreed condition.
  • If a promised repair wasn't done, you can renegotiate closing or walk away (if you have inspection contingencies).

The Bottom Line

Most first-time buyer mistakes are about being unprepared or uninformed. Get pre-approved early, keep your credit and finances stable, shop multiple lenders, and don't make big changes until after closing. The process is straightforward once you know what to avoid.
