Mortgage pre-approval: what it is and why you need it

May 21, 2026 • Michael Miller

Pre-approval is one of the smartest first steps in home buying. It gives you clear financial boundaries, shows sellers you're serious, and sets you up for a faster closing. Here's what you need to know.

What is pre-approval?

Pre-approval is a lender's preliminary assessment of how much you can borrow. They'll review your credit score, income, debt, and assets—then issue a letter stating your approved loan amount (often $250,000 to $500,000+ depending on your profile). This is different from pre-qualification, which is just a rough estimate without verification.

Why it matters before you shop

  • Know your budget: You'll shop for homes within your actual range, not dream bigger and get disappointed.
  • Stronger offers: Sellers see your pre-approval letter and know you're a serious buyer, not "just looking." In competitive markets, this can be the difference between your offer winning or losing.
  • Faster closing: Much of the verification is already done. Your appraisal, title search, and final underwriting move quicker.
  • Negotiating power: You can often ask the seller to help with closing costs or repairs because you're a lower-risk buyer.

What documents you'll typically need

  • Pay stubs or proof of income (recent 2 months)
  • W-2s or tax returns (usually last 2 years)
  • Bank statements (typically last 2-3 months)
  • Proof of down payment funds (where the money is coming from)
  • ID and Social Security number (for credit check)
  • List of current debts (credit cards, car loans, student loans)

How long does it take?

Most lenders can approve you within 24–48 hours if you have your documents ready. Some do it same-day. The pre-approval letter is usually good for 60–90 days, so if you're serious about buying within that window, get it done now.

What affects your approval amount?

Lenders look at your debt-to-income ratio (usually want to see you spending no more than 43% of gross income on housing and other debts), your credit score (620+ for FHA, 620-680 for conventional depending on down payment), and your down payment savings. If you have a 20% down payment ready, you'll likely qualify for more than with 5–10% down.

One important note: Getting pre-approved doesn't guarantee final approval. Things can change between pre-approval and closing—a major purchase, a job change, or new debt can affect your final numbers. Keep your finances stable during the home-buying process.
