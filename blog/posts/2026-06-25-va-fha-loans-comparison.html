VA vs FHA loans: which is right for you?

June 25, 2026 · 6 min read

If you're planning to buy your first home, you've probably heard about FHA loans. If you're a veteran, you've definitely heard about VA loans. But which one should you choose? Let's compare these two powerful options side by side.

VA loans: the veteran advantage

VA loans are available exclusively to eligible veterans, active-duty service members, and surviving spouses. They're arguably the most borrower-friendly mortgages available.

  • Down payment: 0% – You can finance the entire home value with no money down
  • Interest rates: Often the lowest available, sometimes 0.25-0.5% below conventional rates
  • Mortgage insurance: None. Not required at any loan amount
  • Credit score: No minimum requirement, though most lenders want 580+
  • Debt-to-income ratio: Flexible, sometimes up to 50% or higher
  • Funding fee: One-time fee (typically 1.25-2.3% of loan amount) paid at closing or rolled into the loan

FHA loans: the first-timer favorite

FHA loans are backed by the Federal Housing Administration and available to anyone who meets basic requirements. They're designed to help first-time buyers and those with lower credit or income.

  • Down payment: 3.5% minimum (some programs go lower)
  • Interest rates: Slightly higher than VA, typically 0.3-0.5% above conventional rates
  • Mortgage insurance: Required. Your monthly payment includes FHA insurance
  • Credit score: 580+ for 3.5% down; 500-579 for 10% down
  • Debt-to-income ratio: Generally up to 43%, sometimes 50% with strong compensating factors
  • Upfront fee: 1.75% mortgage insurance premium (can be rolled into loan)

Head-to-head comparison: the real costs

Let's say you're buying a $300,000 home with a 30-year loan at 6.8%.

VA Loan:

  • Down payment: $0
  • Funding fee (2%): $6,000 (rolled into loan)
  • Loan amount: $306,000
  • Monthly payment: ~$2,030

FHA Loan:

  • Down payment: $10,500 (3.5%)
  • Upfront insurance (1.75%): $5,062 (rolled into loan)
  • Loan amount: $294,562
  • Monthly payment (includes insurance): ~$2,050

If you're a veteran with no cash saved, the VA loan wins—you put down nothing and the payments are similar or lower. If you're a civilian with some savings, the FHA loan works well. If you have 20% down, a conventional loan beats both on rate and total cost.

The mortgage insurance question

VA loans: No mortgage insurance, ever. This is a huge long-term advantage.

FHA loans: You pay mortgage insurance for the life of the loan if you put down less than 10%, or for at least 11 years if you put down 10%+. This can cost $100-300+ per month, depending on loan size and credit score.

When to choose VA

  • You're a veteran or active-duty service member (or eligible surviving spouse)
  • You have little or no down payment saved
  • You want the absolute lowest long-term cost
  • You value the guarantee of no mortgage insurance

When to choose FHA

  • You're not VA-eligible
  • You have 3.5-10% down saved
  • You're a first-time buyer with lower credit scores (580-620 range)
  • You plan to refinance into a conventional loan within a few years

Don't forget: you can refinance

If you start with an FHA loan and improve your credit or save more equity, you can refinance into a conventional or VA loan later and eliminate mortgage insurance. Many first-timers use FHA as a stepping stone, then upgrade to better terms within 2-3 years.

Final thoughts

If you qualify for a VA loan, it's hard to beat—no insurance, excellent rates, and maximum flexibility. If you don't, FHA is an excellent pathway into homeownership. Compare actual quotes from multiple lenders; rates vary, and getting competitive bids can save you thousands.
