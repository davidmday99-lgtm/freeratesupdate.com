VA vs FHA loans: which is right for you?

June 25, 2026 · 6 min read

Both VA and FHA loans are government-backed programs designed to help borrowers with limited down payments access homeownership. But they have very different eligibility requirements, benefits, and ideal use cases. Let's break down the key differences so you can determine which is right for your situation.

What is an FHA loan?

FHA loans are backed by the Federal Housing Administration and designed for borrowers who struggle to qualify through conventional lending. They're popular with first-time homebuyers and those with lower credit scores.

  • Down payment: As low as 3.5% of the purchase price
  • Credit score: Can qualify with scores as low as 580 (some down to 500 with manual underwriting)
  • Debt-to-income ratio: Up to 43%, sometimes higher with compensating factors
  • Mortgage insurance: Required (both upfront and annual premiums)

What is a VA loan?

VA loans are available exclusively to eligible veterans, active-duty service members, and surviving spouses. They're a powerful benefit that offers terms unavailable anywhere else in the lending market.

  • Down payment: 0% down (true zero-down financing)
  • Credit score: Typically 580 or higher (varies by lender)
  • Funding fee: 2.3% of loan amount (roughly, varies by service history)
  • Mortgage insurance: Not required, ever

Eligibility comparison

Who qualifies for FHA loans: Any U.S. citizen or permanent resident with valid credit and stable income. No military service required. First-time homebuyers are strongly preferred.

Who qualifies for VA loans: Veterans (active or retired), active-duty service members, and surviving spouses with a COE (Certificate of Eligibility). Service time requirements vary by era.

Interest rates: FHA vs VA

VA rates are typically lower than FHA rates, often by 0.25%-0.5%. This reflects the lower risk profile of VA borrowers (military background checks, stable employment patterns). Over a 30-year loan, this difference adds up quickly.

Example: $300,000 loan at 6.25% (FHA) vs 5.75% (VA) = $36,000+ difference in total interest paid.

Insurance and fees: the critical difference

FHA mortgage insurance: Upfront mortgage insurance premium (UFMIP) of 1.75% plus annual premiums (0.55%-0.8% depending on LTV and loan term). This increases your monthly payment and total cost.

VA funding fee: A one-time fee (2.3% for most borrowers) that can be rolled into your loan. Unlike FHA insurance, this fee doesn't recur annually. Many first-time VA buyers qualify for a reduced fee (0%) if discharged due to disability.

Loan limits and areas

FHA loans: No area limit, but maximum loan amounts vary by location and property type. Most markets max out around $500,000-$800,000.

VA loans: No enforced limit for VA-eligible borrowers (after 1993), though individual lenders may set their own limits. You get the full benefit of your entitlement.

Property requirements

FHA loans: Strict property standards. Home must pass FHA appraisal with no major safety issues. Some properties (condos, townhomes) may not qualify or face additional requirements.

VA loans: Similar appraisal standards, but generally more flexible on property type. VA loans can finance condos, townhomes, and manufactured homes more easily than FHA.

Debt-to-income ratios

FHA: Up to 43% DTI (can go higher with compensating factors like significant cash reserves or strong credit history).

VA: Up to 41% DTI typically, but VA loans often allow higher ratios with lender approval, especially for strong borrowers.

Should you choose FHA or VA?

Choose FHA if: You're not military-eligible, need a low down payment, have limited credit history, or are a first-time homebuyer with modest credit.

Choose VA if: You're eligible (veteran, active duty, or surviving spouse). VA loans almost always outperform FHA in rate and cost.

Can you refinance between programs?

Yes. If you have an FHA loan and qualify for a VA loan later (after military service), you can refinance. Conversely, some VA borrowers refinance to conventional loans once they've built equity. Always calculate break-even points before switching programs.
