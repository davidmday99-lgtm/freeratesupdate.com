Refinancing strategies: when it makes sense

May 23, 2026 • Michael Miller

Refinancing isn't always a win—but when it's the right move, it can save you tens of thousands of dollars. Here's how to know if it makes financial sense for you.

The basics: what triggers a refi?

You refinance when rates drop enough to offset closing costs, or when your financial situation improves—better credit, more equity, or a switch in goals (like locking in a shorter term). Most people see the biggest benefit in the first 3-5 years of their loan, when interest makes up the bulk of monthly payments.

The break-even calculation.

Closing costs for a refi typically run 2–5% of your loan balance. With a $300,000 mortgage, that's $6,000 to $15,000. If your new payment saves you $200 per month, you'll need 30–75 months to recoup those costs. If you plan to stay in the home longer than that, a refi probably makes sense. If you're selling in two years, it doesn't.

Rate drop thresholds.

Historically, a 0.5% to 0.75% rate reduction is the inflection point—enough savings to justify the effort and fees. In today's market, if you got a 30-year mortgage at 6.8% and rates fall to 6.0% or lower, you're looking at real monthly savings. Run the numbers with your lender to confirm.

Strategic refinancing moves.

  • Shortening your term: Refinance from 30 years to 15 years to pay off faster and save on total interest—even if your rate stays the same or rises slightly, the payoff often justifies it if cash flow allows.
  • Cashing out equity: A cash-out refi lets you borrow against your home's value for renovations, debt consolidation, or large expenses. Rates are usually better than personal loans or credit cards.
  • Fixing your rate type: If you have an ARM (adjustable-rate mortgage) and rates are climbing, locking into a fixed rate now protects you from future increases.
  • Removing PMI: If you've built 20% equity and your credit improved, you may qualify to drop mortgage insurance—instantly lowering your monthly payment.

Watch out for these pitfalls.

Don't let low rates alone sway you. Beware of extending your loan term to lower payments—you'll pay more in total interest. Also, if you're considering a cash-out refi, make sure you're borrowing for something that adds value or reduces higher-interest debt. And always lock your rate in writing before closing; rate locks typically expire if you don't fund within 30–45 days.

The takeaway.

Refinancing makes sense when the numbers work: lower rates, lower costs, or strategic goals you're confident about. Take time to compare offers, run the break-even math, and ask lenders about no-cost or low-cost options if closing fees are holding you back.
