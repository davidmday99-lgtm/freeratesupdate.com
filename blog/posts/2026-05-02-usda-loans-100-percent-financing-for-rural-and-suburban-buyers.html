USDA loans: 100% financing for rural and suburban buyers

Saturday, May 2, 2026 • Michael Miller

If you're looking to buy in a rural or suburban area and don't have a down payment saved, USDA loans are one of the best-kept secrets in mortgage lending. Here's what you need to know.

  • Zero down payment: USDA loans require no down payment—you can finance 100% of the purchase price (or even slightly more for closing costs).
  • Location matters: The property must be in a USDA-eligible rural or suburban area. Check eligibility at rd.usda.gov. Most areas outside major cities qualify.
  • Income limits apply: Household income typically can't exceed 115% of the area median (varies by county). This is actually a feature—it targets buyers who need the help most.
  • Mortgage insurance: Like FHA, USDA loans include mortgage insurance (called a funding fee and annual insurance). Plan on roughly 0.8% annually, but it drops off after 10 years if your LTV is below 80%.
  • Credit score flexibility: Some lenders work with borrowers at 640+, though 680+ gives you better rate options.

The real advantage: Compare a USDA loan to putting 3% down on an FHA. With USDA, you're not carrying mortgage insurance forever on that small down payment. You also get more predictable terms and no upper limit on the loan amount (unlike FHA caps, which vary by county).

Practical example: Say you're buying a $320,000 home in a USDA-eligible area with a 680 credit score. USDA rates today are competitive with conventional loans—you might lock 6.5% on a 30-year fixed. With zero down, your payment is roughly $2,025/month (including taxes, insurance, and mortgage insurance). With a conventional loan and 3% down, you'd pay ~$1,975/month on the loan itself, but carry PMI of $200+/month. The USDA path is simpler and often cheaper.

Things to watch: USDA approval takes a bit longer (7–10 business days typical). Make sure your lender is experienced with USDA guidelines. Also, if you're self-employed or have recent income changes, prepare stronger documentation—USDA is pickier about verification than conventional lenders.
