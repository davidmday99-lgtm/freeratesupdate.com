Self-employed mortgage tips and documentation

June 19, 2026 • Michael Miller

Being self-employed is financially rewarding—until you try to get a mortgage. Lenders love W-2 employees with steady paychecks. Self-employed borrowers must prove their income is real, stable, and likely to continue. Here's how to navigate the process.

Why Self-Employed Mortgages Are Harder

W-2 employees have simple income verification: pay stubs and W-2s. Self-employed income is more complex:

  • Income fluctuates month to month and year to year
  • Business expenses reduce taxable income (great for taxes, bad for mortgage qualification)
  • Business and personal finances often mix
  • Multiple income streams complicate documentation

Lenders must work harder to verify that your income is real and sustainable.

Definition: Who Is Self-Employed?

For mortgage purposes, you're self-employed if:

  • You own 25% or more of a business
  • You receive 1099 income as your primary compensation
  • You're a freelancer, contractor, or gig worker
  • You're a sole proprietor, LLC member, S-corp or C-corp shareholder

Even if you consider yourself an employee of your own company, lenders see you as self-employed.

The 2-Year Rule

Most lenders require 2 years of self-employment history in the same line of work. Exceptions exist:

  • 1 year may be acceptable if you have 2+ years in the same field as an employee before starting your business
  • Strong compensating factors (large down payment, excellent credit, significant reserves) can help
  • Some non-QM lenders accept 1 year of self-employment with bank statements

Documentation Required

Prepare to provide significantly more documentation than W-2 employees:

Personal Documents:

  • 2 years of personal tax returns (all pages, all schedules)
  • 2 months of bank statements (personal accounts)
  • Government-issued ID

Business Documents:

  • 2 years of business tax returns (1120, 1120S, or 1065 depending on entity type)
  • Year-to-date profit and loss statement
  • Business license or registration
  • CPA letter or accountant-prepared financials (sometimes requested)
  • 2-3 months of business bank statements

How Lenders Calculate Self-Employed Income

Lenders average your income over 2 years using your tax returns—specifically, your adjusted gross income after business deductions.

The Problem: You legitimately deduct business expenses to minimize taxes. But those deductions reduce your qualifying income for the mortgage.

What Gets Added Back:

  • Depreciation: Non-cash expense, typically added back to income
  • Depletion: Similar to depreciation for natural resources
  • Business use of home: Usually added back
  • One-time losses: If documented as non-recurring

What Hurts Your Qualification:

  • Declining income year-over-year (red flag)
  • Negative business income
  • Large business losses on Schedule E
  • Excessive write-offs that reduce net income to near zero

Example Income Calculation:

Year 1 net income: $85,000. Year 2 net income: $95,000. Add-back depreciation: $8,000/year.

Qualifying income: ($85,000 + $8,000 + $95,000 + $8,000) ÷ 24 months = $8,167/month

Bank Statement Loans: The Alternative

If tax returns don't show enough income, bank statement loans offer an alternative:

  • Qualify based on 12-24 months of bank deposits instead of tax returns
  • Lender calculates income from deposit patterns
  • Higher rates than conventional (typically 1-2% higher)
  • Larger down payments required (usually 10-20%)
  • Available through non-QM lenders

Best for: High-earning self-employed borrowers whose tax returns show minimal income due to aggressive deductions.

Strategies to Improve Your Qualification

1. Plan Ahead (12-24 Months Before Buying)

  • Reduce deductions strategically—show more income on tax returns
  • Work with your CPA to balance tax savings vs. mortgage qualification
  • Avoid major business investments that create large depreciation

2. Separate Business and Personal Finances

  • Maintain separate bank accounts
  • Don't co-mingle funds
  • Makes documentation cleaner and underwriting easier

3. Document Everything

  • Keep organized financial records
  • Use accounting software (QuickBooks, FreshBooks)
  • Have year-to-date financials ready on demand

4. Build Strong Compensating Factors

  • Higher down payment (20%+ if possible)
  • Strong credit score (740+)
  • Significant cash reserves (6-12 months)
  • Low debt-to-income ratio

5. Get a CPA Letter

  • Letter from your accountant confirming business viability
  • Helps explain any income irregularities
  • Lenders find comfort in third-party verification

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Switching business structures: Don't change from sole proprietor to S-corp mid-application
  • Starting new businesses: New business income typically can't be used for 2 years
  • Large cash deposits: Unexplained deposits create documentation headaches
  • Tax extensions: If you filed extensions, you'll need to explain why and provide returns when filed
  • Amended returns: Amended returns close to mortgage application look suspicious

Loan Programs for Self-Employed Borrowers

  • Conventional loans: Standard option with full documentation
  • FHA loans: Same 2-year requirement but more flexible on credit
  • Bank statement loans: 12-24 months of deposits instead of tax returns
  • Asset depletion loans: Use liquid assets to calculate imputed income
  • Profit & loss only loans: CPA-prepared P&L without tax returns (rare)

Working with the Right Lender

Not all lenders understand self-employed income. Look for:

  • Experience with self-employed borrowers
  • Access to non-QM products if needed
  • Willingness to manually underwrite complex files
  • Clear communication about documentation requirements

The Bottom Line

Self-employed mortgages require more documentation, more planning, and more patience. Start preparing 12-24 months before you plan to buy. Work with your CPA to balance tax strategy with mortgage qualification. Keep impeccable records. And find a lender who understands entrepreneurial income. With the right preparation, self-employed borrowers qualify every day.
