Understanding Your Loan Estimate: A Section-by-Section Guide

Wednesday, April 29, 2026 · Michael Miller · 5 min read

Within three business days of submitting a mortgage application, every lender is required by federal law to send you a Loan Estimate — a standardized 3-page document that spells out the terms, projected payments, and closing costs of your loan. It's one of the most important pieces of paper in the homebuying process, and most people don't fully understand it.

Here's what each section actually means — and what to watch for.

Page 1: The Basics

The top of page 1 covers the loan terms you requested: loan amount, interest rate, whether the rate is fixed or adjustable, and whether there's a prepayment penalty or balloon payment. Two things to check immediately:

  • Loan amount: For a purchase, the loan amount plus your down payment should equal the sale price. If it doesn't, ask why.
  • Interest rate: If the "Can this change?" column says YES, your rate is adjustable. Make sure that's what you asked for.

The Projected Payments table shows your estimated monthly payment broken into principal & interest, mortgage insurance (if any), and estimated escrow for taxes and insurance. This is your real monthly number — not just the P&I payment lenders often lead with.

Page 2: Closing Costs

This is where the real comparison shopping happens. Closing costs are split into three buckets:

  • Origination charges: Fees set by your lender — application, underwriting, origination points. These vary significantly between lenders and are fully negotiable.
  • Services you cannot shop for: Required third-party costs the lender has already selected (appraisal, credit report, flood determination). Compare these across Loan Estimates.
  • Services you can shop for: Title insurance, settlement agent, pest inspection. You can find cheaper providers than the lender's suggested list.

At the bottom of page 2, you'll see Estimated Cash to Close — this is the total you'll need to bring to the closing table, including down payment, closing costs, and any adjustments.

Page 3: Comparisons & Contact Info

The Comparisons section is underused by most buyers. It shows your APR (which includes fees, not just the rate), Total Interest Percentage over the life of the loan, and the total you'll pay in principal + interest over 5 years. This makes it easy to compare offers apples-to-apples even when lenders structure their fees differently.

The #1 Rule: Get Multiple Estimates

A Loan Estimate is not a commitment from the lender — and getting one doesn't obligate you either. Request estimates from at least 2–3 lenders on the same day so you're comparing the same rate environment. Focus your comparison on origination charges, APR, and cash to close. Small differences in those numbers can mean thousands of dollars.

📋 CFPB Interactive Tool: The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau has a free Loan Estimate explainer where you can click any section of a sample form and get a plain-English explanation of what it means. Worth bookmarking before your next application.
