Down Payment Assistance Programs: What You Need to Know

May 20, 2026 • Michael Miller

The biggest barrier to homeownership for most people isn't the mortgage—it's saving up enough cash for the down payment. But here's what many buyers don't realize: there are real programs designed to help you get into a home with less upfront money.

The reality: You don't always need 20% down. Many programs work with 3% to 5%, or even zero down in some cases. The gap between what you have and what you need? Down payment assistance can bridge that.

What types of programs exist?

  • Forgivable loans. Borrow money for your down payment, then the loan forgives (disappears) over 5–10 years if you stay in the home. No payments upfront; the balance simply reduces each year.
  • Grants. Free money you don't repay. Typically for low-to-moderate income buyers. Limited availability, but they exist in most states.
  • Employer programs. Some major employers (tech, healthcare, government) offer down payment help as a benefit. Check with HR—many people don't know this exists.
  • Non-profit programs. Community organizations often run assistance initiatives. Habitat for Humanity and local land trusts frequently offer below-market programs.
  • Lender programs. Your mortgage lender may offer their own down payment assistance as a selling point. Always ask.

State and local programs matter most. The big federal options (FHA with lower minimums, VA loans with zero down) are well-known. But state-specific programs vary wildly. California's CalHFA, Texas's HOME programs, and Florida's initiatives offer thousands in help. New York, Pennsylvania, Ohio—nearly every state has something. The trick? Finding which one applies to you.

How they work in practice. Say you're buying a $350,000 home and want to put down 5% ($17,500), but you only have $8,000 saved. An assistance program covers the $9,500 gap—either as a forgivable loan or grant. You get the mortgage, move in, build equity, and either have zero payments on the assistance (grant) or watch the balance drop annually (forgivable loan).

The catch.** These programs come with income limits (usually $60,000–$100,000+ depending on your area), occupancy requirements (you must live in the home), and sometimes restrictions on property type or purchase price. Some require credit scores as low as 640; others want 680+. Read the terms carefully.

How to find yours.** Start with your state's Housing Finance Agency. Google "[Your State] HFA down payment assistance." You'll land on the official resource. Then check local nonprofits—a quick search for "down payment help [your city]" surfaces local programs most people miss.

Combine programs.** Here's a pro move: some buyers layer programs. FHA financing + state grant + employer benefit. Not all combinations work, but your lender can run the numbers. It's worth asking.

The bottom line:** If you're worried about down payment cash, don't sit on the sidelines. These programs are designed for people like you. You likely qualify for at least one. Spend 30 minutes researching your state's offerings—it could save you $5,000–$25,000 in cash you'd otherwise need upfront.
