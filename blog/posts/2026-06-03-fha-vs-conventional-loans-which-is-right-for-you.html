FHA vs conventional loans: which is right for you

June 3, 2026 • Michael Miller

The FHA vs conventional question comes down to your down payment, credit history, and how much flexibility you need. Here's what actually matters:

FHA: Lower barriers, more structured costs

  • Down payment: 3.5% minimum (vs 5-20% conventional)
  • Credit score: 580+ can work; some lenders go as low as 500
  • Mortgage insurance (MIP): permanent if you put down less than 10%—this is not temporary
  • Better for: first-time buyers, rebuilding credit, lower down payment situations

Conventional: Stricter upfront, cheaper long-term

  • Down payment: typically 5-20% (lower rates if 20%+)
  • Credit score: 620+ is standard; 740+ gets the best rates
  • PMI (mortgage insurance): drops off automatically once you hit 78-80% LTV
  • Better for: strong credit, decent down payment, long-term payoff

The real math: If you're putting down 3.5%, FHA is almost always cheaper upfront. But if you can get to 5-10% down with conventional, the lack of permanent insurance often saves money over 15-30 years. The break-even point depends on your rate, term, and how long you stay in the home.

Mortgage insurance: where the difference lives

FHA's mortgage insurance premium (MIP) is both upfront (1.75% of loan amount) and annual (0.55% yearly on loans over 95% LTV). It's part of your payment forever unless you refinance to conventional later. Conventional PMI is typically 0.3-1.5% annually and stops automatically. This matters if you plan to stay 10+ years.

Rate impact: FHA and conventional rates are often similar these days, but with your complete profile—credit, income, loan amount—a lender can show you exact numbers. Don't assume one is always cheaper; get pre-approved under both programs.

One practical tip: If you're close to 5% down and have decent credit (620+), run the conventional math. If you're at 3.5% or your credit is lower, FHA often wins. Many buyers benefit from checking both before committing.
