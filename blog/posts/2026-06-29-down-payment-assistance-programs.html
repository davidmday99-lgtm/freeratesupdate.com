Down payment assistance programs: free money for homebuyers

June 29, 2026 · 6 min read

Saving for a down payment is one of the biggest obstacles to homeownership. Fortunately, billions in down payment assistance programs exist—many offering grants that never need to be repaid. The challenge isn't finding programs; it's knowing where to look. Here's your complete guide.

Types of down payment assistance

Not all assistance is the same. Understanding the difference helps you find the best option for your situation.

  • Grants: Free money that doesn't require repayment. True "free money."
  • Forgivable loans: Loans that are forgiven after you live in the home for a set period (often 5-10 years)
  • Below-market loans: Loans at rates significantly below market (subsidized)
  • Matched funds: Programs that match your savings (e.g., for every $1 saved, they contribute $2)
  • Gift funds: Programs allowing gifts from approved sources without counting toward debt

Federal programs

VA loans (for veterans): 0% down payment required. No down payment assistance needed—it's built in. $0 monthly mortgage insurance payments.

FHA loans (for first-time buyers): 3.5% minimum down payment. Qualifying can be easier than conventional loans, making it accessible to many.

USDA loans (rural/suburban areas): 0% down payment in eligible areas. Excellent for rural buyers with moderate income.

HomeReady/Home Possible: Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac programs offering 3% down conventional loans with some assistance available.

State and local programs

Many states and municipalities run their own down payment assistance programs. These vary widely but often include:

  • State housing finance agencies: Most states operate DPA programs. Search "[Your State] housing finance agency"
  • City/county programs: Local down payment grants for residents
  • Employer programs: Some major employers (tech companies, hospitals) offer down payment assistance
  • Non-profit organizations: Groups like Habitat for Humanity, NeighborWorks, and local community development corporations

Employer-sponsored programs

If you work for a larger employer, ask HR about down payment assistance:

  • Tech companies: Google, Microsoft, Meta, and others offer $10,000-$25,000 assistance
  • Healthcare systems: Many hospitals offer programs for employees
  • Government employers: Federal, state, and local government jobs often include benefits
  • Non-profits: Universities and major non-profits frequently have programs

Non-profit and community organizations

Organizations dedicated to homeownership assistance include:

  • NeighborWorks America: Network of local organizations offering down payment assistance and homebuying classes
  • Habitat for Humanity: Builds affordable homes with interest-free down payment assistance
  • Local Community Development Corporations (CDCs): Grassroots organizations with local programs
  • Credit unions: Many offer below-market down payment assistance to members

Gift programs and family assistance sanctioning

Not all down payment assistance is formal. Many borrowers combine multiple sources:

  • Family gifts: Parents, grandparents, or other relatives can gift down payment funds (most programs allow this)
  • Gift letter requirements: Lenders require written confirmation that gifts are indeed gifts, not loans
  • Matched family programs: Some organizations help match family contributions to larger grants

How to find programs in your area

Start with these resources:

  • HUD.gov: Search "down payment assistance" or visit your HUD field office
  • NeighborWorks locator: neighborworks.org has a database of local organizations
  • State housing finance agency: Search "[State name] first time homebuyer programs"
  • Local government: Call your city/county housing department
  • Non-profit networks: Search for local community development organizations

Typical requirements

While programs vary, common requirements include:

  • First-time buyer status: Most programs require you haven't owned in the past 2-3 years
  • Income limits: Usually 80-120% of area median income (though some are higher)
  • Credit score: Minimum 580-640 typically (varies by program)
  • Homebuyer education: Often required; many programs provide free classes
  • Location: You must buy in the program's target area
  • Home price limits: Maximum purchase price varies (usually $300,000-$500,000)

Application timelines

Plan ahead. Applying for down payment assistance isn't instantaneous:

  • Investigation and qualification: 2-4 weeks
  • Underwriting: 2-4 weeks
  • Total time: Expect 4-8 weeks from application to approval
  • Start early: Begin researching programs before you're pre-approved for your mortgage

The catch: don't get scammed

Legitimate down payment assistance is free. Watch out for:

  • Programs charging application fees (legitimate ones are free)
  • Promises of guaranteed approval (red flag)
  • Pressure to pay before you receive funds
  • Requests for upfront fees of any kind

Combining multiple programs

The best strategy often involves stacking programs. Example:

  • Employer program: $15,000
  • State grant: $10,000
  • Family gift: $5,000
  • Your savings: $10,000
  • Total: $40,000 (13% down on $300,000 home without personal debt needed)
