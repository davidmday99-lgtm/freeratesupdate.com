Down payment assistance programs: free money for homebuyers

June 29, 2026 · 7 min read

Most first-time homebuyers think they need to save 10-20% down. Not so. Between FHA loans (3.5% down) and countless down payment assistance programs, many buyers qualify with as little as 0-3% down. Better yet? Some programs provide free money—no repayment required.

Types of down payment assistance

Grants (free money) – You don't repay these. They're gifts from federal, state, or local governments to encourage homeownership.

Forgivable loans – You borrow the money, but the loan is forgiven over time (typically 5-15 years) if you stay in the home.

Second mortgages – You take out two loans: one for the majority of the down payment (the main mortgage) and one for the down payment assistance (the second mortgage).

Deferred payment loans – You borrow down payment money that's paid back only if you sell the home or refinance.

Federal programs

USDA Rural Development Loans – 0% down payment, 2.75% funding fee covered by a grant if you qualify. Available in rural and some suburban areas.

VA Home Loans – 0% down for eligible veterans. No mortgage insurance, excellent rates. If you served, this is your best option.

FHA loans – 3.5% down minimum. Some lenders and nonprofits offer grants to cover part or all of this down payment.

Community Development Block Grants (CDBG) – States and municipalities use federal CDBG funds to offer assistance to low- to moderate-income homebuyers. Programs vary widely by location.

State and local programs

Most states have down payment assistance programs. Here's where to start looking:

  • Your state's housing finance authority: Google "[Your State] Housing Finance Authority." These agencies administer programs.
  • Local nonprofits: Organizations like NeighborWorks and local community development nonprofits often run assistance programs.
  • Your city or county housing office: Many municipalities offer or know about local programs.
  • Employer programs: Some large employers offer down payment assistance to employees (Google, Facebook, Amazon, etc.)

Common eligibility requirements

Most programs have income limits and other criteria:

  • Income: Usually limited to 80-120% of area median income (varies by program)
  • Credit score: Typically 620+ (some programs more flexible)
  • First-time buyer status: Many require you to be a first-time homebuyer (no home ownership in past 3 years)
  • Homebuyer education: Some programs require a homebuyer counseling course (2-4 hours, often free)
  • Home price limits: The home must fall within certain price limits, often median price for the area
  • Location: Some programs are zip-code specific or target underserved communities

Real-world examples (typical assistance amounts)

Scenario 1: First-time buyer, $300,000 home, 80% AMI income

  • FHA loan: 3.5% down ($10,500) + closing costs (~$6,000)
  • State grant program offers: $10,000-15,000 toward down payment
  • Net out-of-pocket: $0-7,500 (loan program, grant, or combination)

Scenario 2: Veteran, same home

  • VA loan: 0% down, covers closing costs through lender credits
  • Net out-of-pocket: $0 (possibly a small funding fee if not waived)

How to find and apply for programs

Step 1: Research – Start with your state's housing finance authority and local nonprofits. Make a list of 5-10 programs you might qualify for.

Step 2: Pre-qualify – Most programs have informal pre-qualification processes. Don't apply yet; just get a sense of what you might qualify for.

Step 3: Work with your lender – Tell your lender you're interested in down payment assistance. Many have partnerships with programs and can guide you.

Step 4: Apply officially – Once you're pre-approved for a mortgage and have a purchase contract, apply for the assistance program. This typically happens in the 30-45 days before closing.

Step 5: Coordinate funding – Your lender and the assistance program will coordinate so the funds arrive at closing.

Things to watch out for

  • Scams: Never pay upfront fees to "secure" down payment assistance or grants. Legitimate programs don't require application fees.
  • Gift letter confusion: Some programs provide gifts (no strings), while others are loans requiring repayment. Understand the difference.
  • Occupancy requirements: Many programs require you to live in the home as your primary residence. Don't violate these.
  • Processing time: Some programs are slow. Start research 4-6 months before buying to allow processing time.

Why programs exist

Governments and nonprofits invest in down payment assistance because homeownership builds wealth and stable communities. They want to help people who want to buy but haven't saved much yet. Take advantage of it—it's not a handout; it's a tool that many qualify for but don't use.

Action items

  • Google "[Your State] down payment assistance programs"
  • Contact your state's housing finance authority
  • Ask your real estate agent if they know of local programs
  • Get pre-approved for a mortgage early so you know your buying power
  • Start your homebuyer education course (often required, always valuable)
