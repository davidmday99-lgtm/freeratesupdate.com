Home equity loans vs HELOCs: which is right for you

May 20, 2026 • Michael Miller

If you own your home outright or have paid down a significant portion of your mortgage, you have access to something many homeowners overlook: equity. That equity can be borrowed against for major expenses, home improvements, or debt consolidation. But there are two main ways to access it—and they work very differently.

What's the difference?

  • Home Equity Loan (HEL): A lump sum of cash, upfront. Fixed rate, fixed payment schedule (typically 5–15 years). You get all the money at once and start making payments immediately.
  • Home Equity Line of Credit (HELOC): A revolving credit line, like a credit card. Variable rate, flexible draws. You only pay interest on what you use. Draw period (typically 10 years), then repayment period (typically 20 years).

When a home equity loan makes sense:

  • You know exactly how much you need (roof repair, major renovation, tuition payment).
  • You want predictability: fixed payment, fixed rate, no surprises.
  • You're comfortable with a defined schedule—payments start now and end at a set date.
  • Rates are favorable and you want to lock in today's numbers.

When a HELOC makes sense:

  • You're not sure of the total amount or timeline (ongoing home projects, variable business needs).
  • You want flexibility: draw what you need, when you need it. Pay only for what you use.
  • You have irregular expenses or upcoming costs that may or may not materialize.
  • You're willing to accept rate variability during the draw period (rates typically adjust quarterly or annually).

The real-world math: Let's say you have $50,000 in equity and need cash for renovation. With a home equity loan, you borrow $50,000 at 7.5% fixed for 10 years—payment roughly $530/month, predictable forever. With a HELOC, you might draw $30,000 initially at prime + 1% (let's say 9.25% in May 2026)—interest-only payment roughly $231/month on that draw. Later, if you need the other $20,000, you draw it. Your rate floats with the market.

Key considerations:

  • Both are secured by your home—failure to pay puts your house at risk.
  • Home equity loans often have lower rates because they're fixed (lenders take less rate risk).
  • HELOCs can be cheaper if you don't use all the money or only need funds for a short time.
  • HELOC interest rates can rise sharply if the Fed raises rates—your payment could jump significantly.
  • Some lenders freeze or reduce HELOC credit lines during market downturns or economic uncertainty.
  • Closing costs exist for both—typically $800–$2,500 depending on your lender and location.

The bottom line: If you need certainty and a clear payoff date, a home equity loan wins. If you need flexibility and want to pay interest only on what you actually use, a HELOC is more efficient. Either way, compare rates and terms from at least 3 lenders—rates vary widely, and the right choice saves you thousands.
