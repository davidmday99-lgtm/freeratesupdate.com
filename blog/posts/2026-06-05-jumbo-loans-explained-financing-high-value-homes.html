Jumbo loans explained: financing high-value homes

June 5, 2026 • Michael Miller

A jumbo loan is any mortgage that exceeds the conforming loan limits set by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac. In 2026, that's $766,550 for most of the U.S. (higher in expensive markets like California and Hawaii). If you're buying above that threshold, here's what changes.

Why jumbo loans are different:

  • Not backed by government-sponsored enterprises (GSEs)—lenders keep the risk
  • Stricter qualification requirements across the board
  • Often require larger down payments (10-20% typical)
  • Higher credit score expectations (700+ is common; 720+ gets better rates)

Typical jumbo loan requirements:

  • Credit score: 700 minimum, 720-740+ for best pricing
  • Down payment: 10-20% (some lenders allow 10% with strong reserves)
  • Debt-to-income ratio: usually capped at 43%, often prefer under 36%
  • Cash reserves: 6-12 months of payments in liquid assets
  • Documentation: extensive income and asset verification (especially for self-employed)

Rate reality: Jumbo rates used to be significantly higher than conforming rates. Today, they're often competitive—sometimes even lower, depending on your profile and the lender. The spread varies by market conditions, so always compare.

The "piggyback" strategy: Some buyers split their financing: a conforming first mortgage up to the limit, plus a smaller second mortgage or HELOC for the rest. This can sometimes result in lower total costs or avoid PMI. The math depends on rates for each loan and your situation.

What lenders really look at:

  • Income stability (W-2 borrowers have an easier path than self-employed)
  • Asset picture—they want to see you're not stretched thin
  • Property type matters: single-family homes are easier than condos or investment properties
  • Loan-to-value ratio: lower LTV = better rates and easier approval

Self-employed borrowers: Expect more documentation—two years of tax returns, profit/loss statements, and possibly a CPA letter. Some lenders offer bank statement programs that look at deposits rather than tax returns, but rates are typically higher.

Practical tip: If you're close to the conforming limit, consider whether a slightly larger down payment could keep you under it. Conforming loans are generally easier to qualify for and may have better terms. But if jumbo is your path, shop multiple lenders—jumbo pricing varies more than conforming.
