FHA loan limits 2026: what buyers need to know

June 14, 2026 • Michael Miller

FHA loans remain one of the most accessible paths to homeownership, especially for first-time buyers. But every year, the Federal Housing Administration updates loan limits that determine how much you can borrow. Understanding these limits—and how they affect your home search—is critical for smart shopping in 2026.

What Are FHA Loan Limits?

FHA loan limits are the maximum amounts the FHA will insure for a mortgage in a given area. These limits vary by county and are based on local median home prices.

  • Floor limit: The minimum limit nationwide, set at 65% of the conforming loan limit.
  • Ceiling limit: The maximum limit in high-cost areas, set at 150% of the conforming loan limit.
  • Special exceptions: Alaska, Hawaii, Guam, and the U.S. Virgin Islands have higher ceiling limits.

2026 FHA Loan Limits by Property Type

FHA limits differ based on the number of units in the property:

  • Single-family (1 unit): Floor $498,257 – Ceiling $1,149,825
  • Duplex (2 units): Floor $637,950 – Ceiling $1,472,250
  • Triplex (3 units): Floor $771,125 – Ceiling $1,779,525
  • Fourplex (4 units): Floor $958,350 – Ceiling $2,211,600

How Limits Are Determined

FHA loan limits are recalculated annually based on changes in median home prices. When home values increase, limits typically rise. The Housing and Economic Recovery Act of 2008 established the formula tying FHA limits to conforming loan limits set by the Federal Housing Finance Agency.

Why Loan Limits Matter

  • Purchasing power: Limits determine the maximum home price you can finance with an FHA loan in your area.
  • Down payment calculation: Your down payment is based on the purchase price, not the limit. But the limit caps what FHA will insure.
  • Market positioning: In high-cost areas, you may need to explore conventional loans if FHA limits don't cover local prices.

High-Cost vs. Low-Cost Areas

Your county's limit depends on whether it's classified as high-cost, low-cost, or somewhere in between:

  • High-cost areas: San Francisco, New York City, Los Angeles—limits are at or near the ceiling.
  • Average-cost areas: Most suburban and mid-tier metro areas fall between floor and ceiling.
  • Low-cost areas: Rural areas and smaller markets typically use the floor limit.

What If Your Home Exceeds the Limit?

If the home you want costs more than your area's FHA limit, you have options:

  • Larger down payment: Pay more upfront to bring the loan amount under the limit.
  • Conventional loan: Switch to a conforming conventional loan with higher limits.
  • Jumbo loan: For homes exceeding conforming limits, jumbo financing is available but requires stronger credit and higher down payments.
  • Adjust your search: Look for homes priced within FHA limits in your target neighborhoods.

FHA Loan Limits vs. Conforming Limits

Don't confuse FHA limits with conforming loan limits:

  • FHA limits: Apply to government-insured FHA mortgages. Generally lower than conforming limits.
  • Conforming limits: Apply to conventional loans backed by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac. Higher ceiling in most markets.

Special Considerations for Multi-Family Properties

FHA loans are popular for house hacking—buying a 2-4 unit property, living in one unit, and renting the others. The higher limits for multi-unit properties make this strategy viable even in competitive markets. Rental income from other units can help you qualify for the mortgage.

How to Check Your Area's Limit

Visit the HUD FHA Mortgage Limits page and enter your state and county. The tool shows current limits for all property types in your area. Bookmark it—limits change annually, and staying informed helps you plan your home search.

Strategy: Maximize FHA Benefits

  • Shop within limits: Focus your search on homes priced to fit FHA financing in your area.
  • Consider neighboring counties: If you're near a county line, check if adjacent areas have higher limits.
  • Plan for appreciation: Buy within limits now; your home's future value isn't restricted by today's limits.
  • Use limits as negotiation leverage: Sellers know FHA buyers have caps. Use this to negotiate prices that work within your financing.

The Bottom Line

FHA loan limits create boundaries, but they're boundaries most buyers never hit. For the majority of home purchases across America, FHA limits are generous enough to cover starter homes and even mid-range properties. Know your area's limits, shop accordingly, and let FHA's flexible requirements—low down payment, lenient credit standards—work in your favor.
