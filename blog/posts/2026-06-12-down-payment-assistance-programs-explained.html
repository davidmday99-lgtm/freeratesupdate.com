Down payment assistance programs explained

June 12, 2026 • Michael Miller

The biggest barrier to homeownership is often the down payment. Saving 5–20% for a down payment takes years. Fortunately, dozens of down payment assistance programs exist to help. Here's what you need to know:

The Big Picture

Down payment assistance comes in three forms:

  • Grants: Free money you don't repay. Often limited, competitive, and income-restricted.
  • Deferred loans: You borrow the down payment but don't pay it back until the main mortgage is paid off or the home sells.
  • 2nd mortgages: You borrow the down payment at market rates. You make monthly payments on this loan.

The Right Mindset: "Down Payment Assistance" Doesn't Mean Free Money

Many programs use the term "assistance," but you often repay the money. Read the fine print:

  • If it's a grant, it's free (and usually competitive or limited).
  • If it's a "forgivable loan," understand when it's forgiven (after X years, if you stay in the home, etc.).
  • If it's a 2nd mortgage, you have two monthly payments.

Government Programs (Your Best Bet for Actual Assistance)

FHA Loans (3.5% Down)

  • FHA loans don't require a large down payment. 3.5% is typical (much lower than conventional 5–20%).
  • You pay FHA mortgage insurance, which costs 0.85% upfront and 0.55–0.8% annually, but 3.5% is affordable for most buyers.
  • Income limits vary by state and family size—check HUD.gov for your area.

VA Loans (0% Down for Veterans)

  • If you're a veteran or active-duty service member, VA loans offer 100% financing with no down payment.
  • No mortgage insurance (a huge advantage).
  • Only qualification: honorable discharge and 181+ days of active duty.

USDA Loans (0% Down for Rural Areas)

  • USDA loans are 0% down financing for homes in rural and some suburban areas.
  • Income limits apply (typically 115% of area median income).
  • A USDA funding fee applies (usually 1–3%, rolled into the loan), but no ongoing mortgage insurance like FHA.

State and Local Down Payment Assistance

Many states and cities offer programs specifically for first-time homebuyers:

  • CalHFA (California Housing Finance Agency): Down payment and closing cost help (up to $25,000 in some programs).
  • New York Housing Finance Agency: First-time buyer programs with down payment grants.
  • Texas Homeownership Program: Grants and favorable financing for eligible buyers.
  • Local housing authorities: Many counties and cities run programs.

Search " down payment assistance program" or visit your state's HFA (Housing Finance Agency) website.

Non-Profit and Community Programs

  • Community Land Trusts: Non-profits that hold land and offer homes at below-market prices (you buy the structure, lease the land).
  • Habitat for Humanity: Builds affordable homes and offers financing help. You often contribute "sweat equity" (volunteer hours). Income limits apply.
  • AmeriVest Properties: Offers grants to qualifying buyers in select states.

Employer Programs

  • Some large employers (Google, Facebook, Microsoft, Amazon) offer down payment assistance ($10,000–$25,000) for employees.
  • Ask your HR department if your employer has a homeownership program.

Employer-Sponsored Loan Programs

  • Some employers partner with lenders to offer favorable down payment loans (lower rates, deferred repayment).

2nd Mortgage Down Payment Assistance

Some lenders offer piggyback loans or 2nd mortgages specifically for down payments:

  • You get a 1st mortgage for 80% of the price and a 2nd mortgage for 10–15% (the down payment).
  • You make payments on both mortgages.
  • The 2nd mortgage rate is higher (5–7%), and you're leveraged higher (more risk).
  • This is cheaper than PMI if the rate is favorable, but still carries debt.

Employer 401k Loans or Withdrawals

  • Some 401k plans allow you to borrow against your balance for a home purchase (first-time buyers can sometimes avoid penalties).
  • You repay yourself (with interest), but the interest goes back to your account.
  • Be careful: if you leave your job, the loan may be due immediately.

Gift Funds from Family

  • Family members can gift down payment money, but you'll need a gift letter stating it's not a loan.
  • Lenders typically allow 100% of the down payment to be a gift (though some want 5–10% of your own funds).
  • No gift tax applies to the giver (as long as it's under annual gift exclusion limits, $18,000 per donor in 2026).

How to Find Programs in Your Area

  • HUD.gov: Search by state for HFA programs and non-profits.
  • Your state's HFA website: Most states publish down payment programs.
  • Local housing authority: County or city housing departments often administer local programs.
  • Ask your real estate agent: Good agents know local programs off the top of their head.
  • Ask your mortgage lender: Lenders often know which programs work with their loans.

Common Eligibility Requirements

  • First-time homebuyer status (varies by program definition).
  • Income limits (usually 80–115% of area median income).
  • Credit score (typically 620+, though 580 is sometimes acceptable).
  • Debt-to-income limits (usually under 45–50%).
  • You must occupy the home as your primary residence.
  • The property must be a single-family, condo, or townhome (not investment property).

Combining Programs

Many buyers stack programs:

  • FHA loan (3.5% down) + state down payment grant (e.g., $5,000) = 0% out of pocket from savings.
  • VA loan (0% down) + closing cost assistance = truly no cash needed, only lender fees covered.

The Bottom Line

Down payment assistance is real and abundant. You might not need to save for 5+ years to buy. Between FHA, VA, USDA, state programs, and community nonprofits, first-time buyers have more options than ever. Start by checking government sites specific to your state and ask your lender which programs you qualify for. The down payment that felt impossible might be achievable.
