Buying a home: what matters most in your mortgage options

Friday, May 22, 2026 • Michael Miller

You've found the home. The seller loves your offer. Now comes the mortgage decision—and honestly, this is where most buyers get confused. They focus on the interest rate alone and miss the bigger picture. Here's what actually matters.

Start with your monthly payment target, not just the rate

A 0.5% lower rate sounds great until you realize it only saves you $75 per month on a $350,000 loan. But if you have to pay $8,000 more in closing costs to get that rate, you're breaking even in 106 months—nearly 9 years. That's too long if you plan to move or refinance sooner. Instead, ask yourself: "What monthly payment can I comfortably afford?" Work backward from there. A slightly higher rate with lower closing costs might be the smarter move for your timeline.

Compare loan types based on your down payment and flexibility

  • Conventional loans: Usually the best rates if you have 10-20% down and solid credit. No mortgage insurance if you put down 20%.
  • FHA loans: Require only 3.5% down but carry mortgage insurance premiums (roughly 0.5-1.0% annually). Great for first-time buyers who don't have a large down payment yet.
  • VA loans: Often zero down, no mortgage insurance. If you're eligible as a veteran, this is usually your best option.
  • USDA loans: Zero down in eligible rural areas. Similar benefits to VA if you qualify.

The "best" loan isn't about the lowest rate—it's about your total costs and what flexibility you need. An FHA loan might have a 0.25% higher rate than conventional, but if it means you can close in 30 days instead of 45, and you get to waive the appraisal, that might win.

Ask about seller credits, points, and cash-to-close

Your lender can offer you points—essentially buying a lower rate by paying more upfront. The math here matters. Paying $3,000 today to lower your rate from 6.5% to 6.2% makes sense if you're keeping the home for 10+ years. If you're buying an investment property and selling in 5 years? Probably not worth it.

Seller credits are underrated. If your offer is competitive but the seller is willing to cover some closing costs, that can save you tens of thousands. Ask your lender what the loan will allow—some loans allow up to 6% of the purchase price as seller contributions.

Get clear on your timeline: pre-approval vs underwriting vs clear-to-close

This is where delays hide. Pre-approval looks good but doesn't trigger appraisals or title searches. Underwriting is where your full financial picture gets reviewed—and where issues often surface. "Clear-to-close" is when everything is locked in and you're 3-5 days from signing. Ask your lender upfront: "What's your average time from pre-approval to close? What typically causes delays?" The honest answer tells you if they're efficient or if you'll be counting down the days.

The bottom line

The mortgage that matters most is the one that fits your payment budget, aligns with your timeline, and doesn't cost you a fortune in closing fees. Don't chase the lowest rate in isolation—you'll miss the real value. Talk to a few lenders, compare their full offers (rate + points + seller credits + timeline), and pick the one that makes sense for your actual situation.
