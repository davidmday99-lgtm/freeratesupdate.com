HELOC vs home equity loan: which is right for you?

June 21, 2026 · 5 min read

Homeowners sitting on significant equity have two popular options for tapping that wealth: a Home Equity Line of Credit (HELOC) or a traditional home equity loan. Both use your home as collateral, but they work very differently—and the right choice depends on your specific financial situation and goals.

What is a home equity loan?

A home equity loan is a lump-sum loan with a fixed interest rate and fixed monthly payments over a set term, typically 5-30 years. You receive all the money upfront and begin repaying immediately.

  • Fixed rate: Your interest rate never changes, making budgeting predictable
  • Lump sum: You get all the money at closing
  • Fixed payments: Same payment every month for the life of the loan
  • Best for: One-time large expenses like home renovations, debt consolidation, or major purchases

What is a HELOC?

A HELOC is a revolving line of credit, similar to a credit card but secured by your home. You're approved for a maximum amount and can draw funds as needed during the "draw period" (typically 10 years), then repay during the "repayment period" (typically 10-20 years).

  • Variable rate: Interest rate fluctuates with the prime rate
  • Flexible access: Draw only what you need, when you need it
  • Interest-only option: Many HELOCs allow interest-only payments during the draw period
  • Best for: Ongoing expenses, emergency reserves, or projects with uncertain costs

Key differences to consider

Interest rates: Home equity loans typically have slightly higher rates than HELOCs initially, but the fixed rate provides protection if rates rise. HELOCs start lower but can increase significantly over time.

Costs: Home equity loans often have higher closing costs. HELOCs may have annual fees or inactivity fees but lower upfront costs.

Flexibility: HELOCs win on flexibility—you only pay interest on what you've borrowed. Home equity loans charge interest on the full amount from day one.

When to choose a home equity loan

  • You know exactly how much you need
  • You want predictable monthly payments
  • You're concerned about rising interest rates
  • You're consolidating high-interest debt and want to lock in savings

When to choose a HELOC

  • You have ongoing or unpredictable expenses
  • You want access to funds for emergencies
  • You're comfortable with variable rates
  • You want to minimize interest costs by borrowing only as needed

Current rate environment

In today's market, the spread between HELOC and home equity loan rates is relatively narrow. If you're leaning toward a HELOC, consider whether the flexibility is worth the interest rate risk. Many lenders now offer HELOCs with rate caps or the option to convert portions to fixed rates.
