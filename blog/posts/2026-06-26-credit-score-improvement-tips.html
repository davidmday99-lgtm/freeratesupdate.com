Boost your credit score: 6 strategies for better mortgage rates

June 26, 2026 · 6 min read

Your credit score is your financial report card—and it directly impacts your mortgage rate. Even a 50-point difference can mean tens of thousands of dollars over the life of your loan. Let's walk through proven strategies to boost your score quickly.

Why your credit score matters so much

A 50-point gap in credit score can change your mortgage rate by 0.5-1%, which translates to roughly $100-200 extra per month on a $300,000 loan. Over 30 years, that's $36,000-72,000 in additional interest. Your credit score isn't just a number—it's money.

Strategy 1: Lower your credit utilization

Credit utilization is how much credit you're using relative to your limits. Maxing out cards screams "financial stress" to lenders. Ideally, use less than 30% of your available credit.

  • If you have a $10,000 credit limit, keep your balance under $3,000
  • Ask your credit card company to increase your limits (if you can do so without a hard inquiry)
  • Pay down balances strategically—prioritize the highest-utilization cards first
  • Don't close old cards after paying them off; available credit helps your score

Timeline to impact: 1-2 months. Utilization changes are reflected in your next statement.

Strategy 2: Make all payments on time

Payment history is 35% of your credit score—the single biggest factor. Even one late payment can drop your score 50-100 points. A missed payment older than 7 years falls off your report, but recent ones hurt badly.

  • Set up automatic payments for at least the minimum amount
  • Pay bills several days early to account for mail delays (or use online payment with confirmation)
  • If you've been late, get current immediately—the damage fades over time

Timeline to impact: Ongoing. Your most recent 24 months matter most. A year of perfect payments meaningfully improves an otherwise spotty history.

Strategy 3: Dispute errors on your credit report

Studies show about 20% of credit reports contain errors—sometimes accounts that aren't yours. If you're seeing inaccurate info, dispute it.

  • Get your free credit report at annualcreditreport.com
  • Look for accounts you don't recognize, wrong balances, or accounts marked late that you paid on time
  • File a dispute with the credit bureau (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) directly online
  • Include documentation (bank statements, payment confirmations, letters)
  • The bureau has 30 days to investigate and respond

Timeline to impact: 30-45 days. Errors removed can bounce your score up 20-100+ points if they were dragging you down.

Strategy 4: Become an authorized user on someone else's account

If a family member or friend has excellent credit, ask to be added as an authorized user on one of their credit cards. Their positive history gets added to your report, which can boost your score instantly (with some card issuers) or within 1-2 months.

  • You don't need to use or even receive the card—you just benefit from their credit behavior
  • Only works if the primary account holder has a good history of on-time payments and low utilization
  • If they later miss payments, it can hurt your score, so choose carefully

Timeline to impact: Instant to 30 days, depending on the card issuer.

Strategy 5: Pay off collections or charge-offs (with caution)

If you have accounts in collections or charge-offs, paying them off is good, but the impact is nuanced. Paid accounts still show up on your report, but newer collection accounts hurt more than old ones.

  • Before paying: Request a "pay for delete" agreement in writing. The collector agrees to remove the account from your report after payment.
  • If they won't agree to that, consider the age of the account. If it's 5+ years old and will fall off soon anyway, paying it might actually lower your score temporarily by activating the account.
  • Recent collections (under 2 years) should be paid off before applying for a mortgage

Timeline to impact: 30-90 days after payment if pay-for-delete is negotiated; varies if the account remains on your report.

Strategy 6: Don't apply for new credit right before buying

Every credit application triggers a hard inquiry, which can drop your score 5-10 points. Multiple inquiries in a short timeframe are even worse. The impact fades after 12 months, but during the mortgage application process, you want your score as high as possible.

  • Stop applying for credit 6 months before buying
  • Don't open new credit cards to "increase available credit"
  • Don't do a hard inquiry just to check your score—use free monitoring services instead

Timeline to impact: Hard inquiries fade after 12 months but affect your score immediately.

How many points can you gain?

Depending on your starting point and how aggressively you work:

  • Lowering utilization: 20-50 points in 1-2 months
  • Fixing errors: 20-100+ points (if significant)
  • Becoming authorized user: 10-50 points instantly to 1 month
  • Paying down debt: Compounds over time with consistent effort
  • 12 months of perfect payments on a poor history: 50-100+ points

Timeline: when to start

If you're planning to buy in the next 6-12 months, start now. Most improvements take time. However, if you pull your report today and find errors, dispute them immediately—those fixes can happen in 30 days. If your utilization is high, paying that down can help within 1-2 months.
