Boost your credit score: 6 strategies for better mortgage rates

June 26, 2026 · 5 min read

Your credit score is one of the most powerful tools you have when buying a home or refinancing. A difference of just 20 points can move you from one rate tier to another—potentially saving or costing you tens of thousands of dollars. Here's what you need to know to get the best score possible before applying for your mortgage.

Strategy 1: Pay down credit card balances

Credit utilization (the percentage of available credit you're using) accounts for about 30% of your credit score. This is the fastest way to boost your score.

  • Aim for under 30% utilization on each card (even better: under 10%)
  • You don't have to pay off balances completely, just reduce them
  • This one step alone can improve your score 10-50 points in 1-2 months
  • Even better: Pay off balances entirely, then keep them paid down

Strategy 2: Make all payments on time (always)

Payment history is 35% of your credit score—the single biggest factor. One late payment can ding your score 100+ points.

  • Autopay is your friend. Set it and forget it
  • Even one day late counts as late (unless 30+ days, which is worse)
  • Old late payments hurt less than recent ones
  • If you do miss a payment, get current within 30 days to minimize damage

Strategy 3: Don't close old credit cards

Multiple factors affect your score: age of accounts (15%), number of new inquiries (10%), and mix of credit types (10%). Closing old cards hurts all three.

  • Keep old cards open even if unused
  • Use them occasionally to show activity
  • The longer your account history, the better
  • Exception: Close cards with high annual fees after paying them off

Strategy 4: Avoid new credit inquiries before applying

Each credit inquiry (hard pull) can temporarily lower your score by 5-10 points. Multiple inquiries accumulate quickly.

  • Pause new credit applications 6 months before mortgage shopping
  • Don't buy a car, open new cards, or take new loans if possible
  • Multiple mortgage inquiries within 14 days count as one (shop quickly)

Strategy 5: Fix errors on your credit report

Errors happen. About 20% of Americans have errors on their credit reports. Pull your report and dispute any inaccuracies.

  • Get your free report at AnnualCreditReport.com
  • Check for wrong payment history, accounts you didn't open, or incorrect balances
  • Dispute errors in writing; most agencies respond within 30 days
  • Correcting errors can improve your score 10-100+ points

Strategy 6: Build credit diversity (if needed)

Credit mix (10% of your score) includes credit cards, installment loans, auto loans, and mortgages. If you only have credit cards:

  • Consider a small installment loan or becoming an authorized user on another account
  • This is a longer-term play (6-12 months to show history)
  • But having diverse credit types helps when lenders review your profile

Timeline expectations

How long does improving your score take? It depends on your starting point:

  • Quick gains (1-2 months): Paying down balances, fixing report errors
  • Medium-term (3-6 months): Consistent on-time payments, new inquiries aging off
  • Long-term (6-12 months): Building credit history, removing late payments from your report

The mortgage impact

Here's the real motivation: What does a 20-point improvement mean for your mortgage?

  • 740+ credit score: Best rates available
  • 700-739: Good rates, competitive terms
  • 660-699: Acceptable rates, but significant gaps from optimal
  • 620-659: Limited lender options, higher rates

Moving from 660 to 700 (40 points) might improve your rate from 6.75% to 6.25%—saving $63/month or $22,680 over 30 years on a $300,000 loan.

Don't rush

If you have 6+ months before buying, use the time to improve your score. Small improvements now compound into significant rate savings later. If you need to buy sooner, focus on the quick wins (paying down balances, fixing errors).
