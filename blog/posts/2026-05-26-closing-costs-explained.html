Closing costs explained

May 26, 2026 • Michael Miller

Closing costs are the fees and expenses you pay at the very end of a home purchase or refinance—they're separate from your down payment. Most buyers expect them to be 2–5% of the loan amount, which means on a $300,000 mortgage, you could owe $6,000 to $15,000 at closing.

What's actually in closing costs?

  • Loan origination fees: The lender's fee to process and approve your loan, usually 0.5–1% of the loan amount.
  • Appraisal fee: The cost to have the home valued, typically $400–600.
  • Title search and insurance: Ensures you're buying from the actual owner and protects against future claims—usually $600–1,000 combined.
  • Inspection and survey: Optional but recommended; your inspector or surveyor gets paid here.
  • Property taxes and homeowners insurance: Your lender collects 2–6 months upfront to set up an escrow account.
  • HOA transfer fees: If applicable, paid to the homeowners association.
  • Attorney or title company fees: Varies by state; some states require attorney involvement in every closing.

Pro tip: Ask for a Loan Estimate within 3 days. By federal law, your lender must provide a detailed estimate. Review every line. Some fees are negotiable—especially loan origination fees and title insurance quotes. Don't be shy; lenders often have room to move.

Can you reduce closing costs? Yes. Here's what actually works:

  • Shop around. Title insurance and appraisals can vary 10–20% between providers.
  • Ask the seller to cover some costs as part of negotiation. This happens more often than you'd think, especially in competitive markets.
  • Compare lender quotes side-by-side. A 0.5% origination fee difference on a $300,000 loan is $1,500—real money.
  • Skip optional services you don't need. Some inspections or surveys might not apply to your situation.

What about refinancing? Refi closing costs are typically lower—1–3% of the loan amount—because you're skipping the appraisal and title insurance. But they still add up. Make sure your monthly payment savings justify the upfront cost.

The most important thing: Never sign closing documents blind. Read the final Closing Disclosure 3 days before closing. It lists every dollar you're paying. If something doesn't match your Loan Estimate from earlier, ask why.

Closing costs are real, but they're not a surprise if you plan ahead. Know what you're paying for, shop around, and negotiate. Your lender works for you—treat it that way.
