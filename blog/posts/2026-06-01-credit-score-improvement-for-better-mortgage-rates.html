Credit score improvement for better mortgage rates

June 1, 2026 • Michael Miller

Your credit score is one of the most powerful tools in mortgage qualification. A 50-point difference can mean $10,000 or more in interest over the life of your loan. Here's how to improve it before you apply.

Why it matters:

  • A 620 score might qualify you for 6.8%; a 740 could be 6.2%—that's half a percent, worth real money.
  • Higher scores unlock better loan programs and lower fees.
  • Lenders pull "soft" inquiries during pre-approval; most don't hurt your score.

Fast wins (30–60 days):

  • Pay down revolving balances. Credit card utilization (how much of your limit you're using) makes up 30% of your score. Getting below 30% utilization can add 20–30 points quickly. Paying off a $5,000 balance on a $10,000 card goes from 50% to 0% utilization.
  • Make all payments on time. A single late payment can drop your score 50–100 points. If you have older late payments, they hurt less over time, but stay on-time moving forward.
  • Correct errors on your report. Pull your free credit report at AnnualCreditReport.com. Dispute any wrong accounts, incorrect balances, or fraudulent inquiries with the bureaus directly.

Medium-term moves (3–6 months):

  • Ask for credit limit increases. Some issuers do soft inquiries (no score impact). A higher limit with the same balance instantly improves your utilization ratio.
  • Keep old accounts open. Length of credit history is 15% of your score. Closing old cards can hurt; keep them active with small purchases if possible.
  • Diversify your credit mix. Lenders like to see installment loans (auto, personal) mixed with revolving credit (cards). If you only have credit cards, a small auto loan or credit-builder loan can help (though the inquiry will temporarily lower your score 5–10 points).

Longer timeline (6–12 months):

  • Build positive history. Every month on-time payment adds to your score. If you're rebuilding from poor credit, consistent payment history is your best asset.
  • Limit new inquiries. Hard inquiries (from loan applications) drop your score 5–10 points each. If you're shopping for rates, do it within 14 days—most lenders count multiple inquiries as one if they're close together.

Red flags to avoid:

  • Don't open new credit accounts right before applying for a mortgage.
  • Don't close old cards to improve utilization—keep them open.
  • Don't stop paying on other debts to pay down credit cards.
  • Don't assume you need a perfect score. Many lenders approve 620+ scores; many offer competitive rates at 700+.

The math: how much does your score actually save you? On a $300,000 mortgage at 6.5%, your monthly payment is about $1,896. At 6.0%, it's $1,799—a $97 savings per month, or $34,920 over 30 years. For many people, spending a few weeks improving their score and waiting for the updates to post is absolutely worth it.
