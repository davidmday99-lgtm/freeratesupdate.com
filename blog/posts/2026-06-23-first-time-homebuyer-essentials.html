First-time homebuyer essentials: don't skip these steps

June 23, 2026 · 7 min read

Buying your first home is thrilling—and overwhelming. Between choosing neighborhoods, making offers, and navigating mortgage processes, it's easy to miss critical details. Let's walk through the essentials to help you get it right the first time.

Step 1: Check your credit and handle the essentials

Before you even talk to a lender, pull your credit report and fix any errors. Most lenders require a credit score of at least 580 for FHA loans or 620+ for conventional mortgages. If you're below that, spend 3-6 months paying down debt and making on-time payments.

  • Pull your free credit report at annualcreditreport.com
  • Dispute any errors immediately
  • Avoid opening new credit accounts or making large purchases before applying
  • Pay down high credit card balances (aim for under 30% utilization)

Step 2: Get pre-approved (not just pre-qualified)

Pre-qualification is a ballpark estimate. Pre-approval is serious—the lender verifies your income, assets, and credit. It shows sellers you can actually close. In today's competitive market, pre-approval isn't optional.

Expect to provide: recent tax returns, 2 months of pay stubs, 2 months of bank statements, and employment verification. Yes, it's a lot. Yes, it matters.

Step 3: Understand your mortgage options

You have choices: FHA, conventional, VA (if eligible), USDA (if eligible), or portfolio loans. Each has pros and cons. FHA loans accept lower credit scores and down payments (3.5%) but charge mortgage insurance. Conventional loans have stricter requirements but lower insurance costs at 20% down.

  • FHA: 3.5% down, flexible credit, higher insurance
  • Conventional: 3-20% down, better credit needed, lower insurance at 20%
  • VA: 0% down, no insurance, for military/veterans
  • USDA: 0% down, 2.75% down payment assistance, for rural areas

Step 4: Save for your down payment (and closing costs)

Many first-timers focus on the down payment but forget closing costs. While down payments range from 0-20%, closing costs typically run 2-5% of the home's price. That means on a $300,000 home with 5% down, you need $15,000 down plus $6,000-$15,000 in closing costs—a total of $21,000-$30,000 cash.

Research down payment assistance programs in your state. Many offer grants, not loans, to help qualifying first-time buyers.

Step 5: Get the mortgage fundamentals right

Fixed-rate mortgages are predictable. ARMs (Adjustable Rate Mortgages) start low but can increase after the initial period. For first-timers, a 30-year fixed rate offers peace of mind. You can always pay extra toward principal if rates drop later.

Step 6: Find a good real estate agent and lender

This is not the place to skimp. Interview 2-3 agents and 2-3 lenders. Ask about their process, timeline, and how they'll communicate with you. A good team catches problems early and saves you money.

Step 7: Get a home inspection and appraisal

Once your offer is accepted, hire an independent home inspector. This costs $300-500 and can save you thousands by catching structural issues, roof problems, or HVAC failures. Your lender will order an appraisal to confirm the home's value matches the purchase price.

Common first-time buyer mistakes to avoid

  • Don't make large purchases before closing: A new car or furniture can change your debt-to-income ratio and kill your approval.
  • Don't switch jobs right before buying: Lenders want employment stability. If you must switch, ensure it's an internal promotion or lateral move in the same field.
  • Don't cosign for anyone: Their debt becomes your debt in the lender's eyes.
  • Don't overspend on the home: Just because you're approved for $400k doesn't mean you should borrow it. Budget for property taxes, insurance, and maintenance.

Final thoughts

Buying a home is a marathon, not a sprint. Take time to understand your options, ask questions, and avoid rushing. A few months of preparation can save you years of regret—and tens of thousands of dollars.
